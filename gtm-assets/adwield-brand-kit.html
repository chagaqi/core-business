1. NAME + MEANING

Adwield = ad + wield.

  • ad — the product: AI-native ad creative.
  • wield — the posture: you don't hire this, you wield it. A weapon in your hand, under your command, swung by you or by an operator you equip.

The name does one job: it reframes the buyer from client (passive, managed, billed to wait) into wielder (in command of a weapon). That single reframe is the whole brand. Every agency in the inbox sells you a service you submit to; Adwield sells you a weapon you command.

Pronunciation / styling: "AD-weeld." Always one word, capital A: Adwield. Never "Ad Wield," "AdWield," or "ADWIELD" in body copy (all-caps only for logo lockups). Domain: adwield.com.

The two readings of "wield" (both intended):

  • We wield it for you (Done-For-You — the Maxed-Out Knight).
  • You wield it yourself (Done-With-You — the Forge install). (A third, fully-self-serve software reading exists internally but is not for front-end copy — see the Front-End Copy Rule above and §5 STEP 3.)

2. THE BIG IDEA

You don't hire an agency. You equip a weapon.

That is the entire positioning in six words. Hold it above everything.

Why it works: the buyer (a $20–100K/mo functional-consumables founder) has been burned by an agency that billed him to be slow and never learned his brand (doc 12, Element 3). "Hire an agency" is a scar. "Equip a weapon" is the opposite motion — fast, owned, under his command, swung on his schedule. The frame does the objection-handling before a single feature is named.

The flavor that carries it: MMO/RPG — over proof-only substance. Think a tasteful RPG-HUD/crest aesthetic. Linear-meets-loadout. A premium SaaS surface with a knowing game-flavored vocabulary layered on top. Never WoW clipart, never cheesy, never a costume. The buyers spend $20–100K/mo; the swagger has to feel earned, which is exactly why the free First Strike and the guarantee (not bravado) carry the proof. Flavor is the wink; proof is the handshake.

The one-line pitch (the Hero Line — see §3): Equip us as your secret weapon — raise your hit rate, land more critical strikes in the ad auction.

The bridge to the real mechanic (say this when flavor needs grounding): every winner you ship dies on a clock — creative fatigue at ~7 weeks (doc 12, Element 5; doc 37). The weapon's whole job is to keep a fresh winner equipped before the last one breaks against The Fatigue Wall. The game vocabulary is just the honest mechanism, named in a way a buyer remembers.

7. THE NO-RISK LOADOUT (the guarantee)

The guarantee is brand-critical: it's the proof that carries the swagger (doc 12 Element 9; doc 27 Trust Ladder rank 2). In-flavor name: The No-Risk Loadout.

The promise (in-flavor framing):
Beat your control or the next cycle's free. We equip you, and the risk stays on us.

The full, locked guarantee language (doc 12 — use verbatim where the guarantee must be precise; the in-flavor line above is the headline, this is the substance):

"Within 30 days, our creative beats your current best ad on hook-rate / hold-rate (3-second + thumbstop) AND your CPA holds flat or better — measured against your own control, in your own account, with the head-to-head data on the table. If it doesn't, your next 24-variation cycle is free. Month-to-month, cancel anytime, and you keep every video either way."

Why this guarantee, brand-side:

  • Hook-rate / hold-rate is creative-controllable (we actually own it) and correlates downstream — not the vanity trap raw CTR is (doc 12).
  • "CPA holds flat or better" neutralizes "high-CTR creative that bled cash."
  • Labor currency (next cycle free), not cash — a small-budget solo can always honor it; it costs no cash (doc 12 Element 7). Never promise a cash/ROAS refund.
  • Month-to-month + cancel-anytime + keep-the-videos kills the counterparty doubt ("will this stranger be here next month?").

Brand rule: the in-flavor headline ("Beat your control or the next cycle's free") may lead; the precise locked language must appear wherever the guarantee is the actual commitment (LP guarantee block, sell sheet, contract). Never let the flavor soften or overstate the real terms.

8. VOICE & TONE RULES

The register: premium-with-a-wink. Gamer-adjacent. Never cheesy. A buyer spending $20–100K/mo must feel the swagger is earned — and the only thing that earns it is proof (the free First Strike + the guarantee), not bravado. The flavor is seasoning on plain, specific, operator-true copy (doc 13 §6) — never a substitute for it.

The test for every line: Would a sharp, skeptical, burned-once founder read this and nod — or wince? If the game flavor makes him wince, cut it. If a metric or claim isn't real, cut it. Confidence comes from specificity, not from slogans.

DO

  • Lead with the weapon frame. "Equip," "wield," "loadout," "crit," "hit rate" — used naturally, where they clarify, not stacked for effect.
  • Stay specific — specificity is the brand (doc 13 §6). 4 angles, 24 variations, every 2 weeks, 7-week fatigue, $20–100K/mo, $497, capped at 5 brands. Never round-number vagueness.
  • Make the winner the hero, volume the method (doc 12 Element 4; doc 13 §6). "Find the next winner before the last one dies" — volume is how the crit gets found faster, never the pitch.
  • Be honest about limits. Name the AI failure mode (consumption "drink-and-react" B-roll) before they do (doc 12 Element 5). Name the real client time cost. Honesty builds the belief the offer is short on.
  • Use earned empathy, never pity (doc 13 §6). "The game changed under you; here's the lever you couldn't pull alone" — never "I know it's been hard."
  • Let the guarantee and the free First Strike carry the credibility. Swagger is backed by risk-on-us, not by claims.
  • Keep the flavor tasteful and load-bearing. A game word earns its place only if it makes the meaning clearer or stickier. Otherwise, plain English.
  • Plain declarative sentences. "Ship 24 ads in two weeks," not "leverage synergistic AI workflows."

DON'T

  • No cheese, no WoW-clipart energy. No "EPIC LOOT!", no "+5 SWORD OF ROAS," no dragon emojis, no Comic-Sans-of-the-soul. The flavor is a tasteful crest, not a costume.
  • No fabricated metrics, ever (see §9). Hit rate and crits are the AIM, never a claimed result.
  • Never lead with volume — it pings the slop alarm (doc 13 §6). Volume is the method.
  • Never lead with price, and never lead with the word "AI" (doc 13 §4). Lead with their First Strike (if priority), then the 7-week truth, then the mechanism, then proof-only credibility + guarantee.
  • No emojis. No exclamation-point selling. Confidence comes from specificity, not hype (doc 13 §6).
  • No banned hype words: revolutionary, game-changing, cutting-edge, synergy, death spiral, "got your nights back," outvariant, "10x" as filler, "effortless," "near-zero effort" (doc 13 §6).
  • Don't sell the community's grief back to them. Reference the post-Andromeda Meta break / 7-week fatigue as shared context, never paste a Reddit quote as a manipulative hook (doc 13 §6).
  • Don't claim an ad track record you don't have (doc 12 Element 9; doc 27). The home-gym business appears at most once, lightly, as character — never as ad-proof.
  • Don't let flavor overstate the offer. If a game metaphor makes the promise sound bigger than the locked offer delivers (creative, not whole-account ROAS), cut the metaphor.

Quick voice swatch (right vs wrong)

  • ✅ "Equip us as your secret weapon — 4 angles, 24 variations, every two weeks, launched and tagged in your own account. Beat your control or the next cycle's free."
  • ❌ "Unleash EPIC ad POWER and 10x your ROAS with our game-changing AI weapon! 🗡️🔥"
  • ✅ "Your last winner is going to break against The Fatigue Wall around week 7. We keep a fresh one equipped before it does."
  • ❌ "Slay the algorithm boss and loot infinite sales!"

9. THE PROOF-ONLY GUARDRAIL (hard rule — governs all of the above)

This is the rule the whole kit bends to. It comes straight from doc 12 (Element 9), doc 13 (§5), and doc 27 (the entire playbook).

Never fabricate metrics, case studies, testimonials, reviews, star ratings, or client logos. Every metric is a target to measure, never a claimed result.

How the flavor stays honest:

  • Hit rate + crits are the AIM and the MECHANIC — the thing we're swinging for — never a fabricated outcome. "Raise your hit rate" is a promise of effort and method; "our hit rate is 80%" (with no real account behind it) is a lie. Never the second one until it's real and measured.
  • Where proof would normally go, use: the free First Strike + the No-Risk Loadout guarantee + "founding / early" framing + a literal [CASE STUDY PLACEHOLDER]. The placeholder stays literal until a real founding-partner delta exists (doc 37 §3). The founding-slot count stays the real current number — never inflated (doc 37).
  • The home-gym business (the founder's real ~$2M offline company) appears at most once, lightly, as character — "a real operator who respects your margin, not an agency middleman" — and never as ad-proof. Never claim it was online, e-commerce, DTC, or that it ran paid ads (doc 27 §6).
  • No claimed ad / paid-ads track record. Trust is earned by demonstration: the First Strike, the teardown portfolio, one honestly-labeled real hold-rate number off the founder's own small test, the founding-partner deltas, and the guarantee (doc 27 Trust Ladder).
  • Sample-host watch analytics are an internal metric only — never quoted externally as proof or traction (doc 37 §3).
  • Zero fabrication, ever. No invented numbers, no fake logos, no "as seen in," no borrowed follower counts. In a niche flooded with fakery, the clean empty inventory is itself a trust signal (doc 27 §7).

The reframe that powers the whole brand: newness isn't the weakness to hide — it's the reason the free First Strike and the guarantee exist. We prove it on your product, before you pay, with the risk on us. Confident and honest, never apologetic (doc 27).

10. VISUAL IDENTITY DIRECTION

The north star: tasteful RPG-HUD / crest. Linear-meets-loadout. A premium, restrained SaaS surface (clean type, generous space, sharp grid — think Linear, Vercel, Raycast) with a subtle game-flavored layer: a crest/sigil mark, a HUD-style stat treatment for the metric glossary, a "loadout"-style card grid for the 4×24. The game is in the details and vocabulary, not in the skin.

Logo / mark

  • A crest or sigil — geometric, modern, monoline or low-relief. Reads as a premium guild mark, not a fantasy-game logo. The "A" of Adwield can anchor it.
  • Wordmark: clean geometric or grotesk sans, tight, confident. Lowercase or small-caps "adwield" for the wordmark; the crest carries the ceremony.

Palette (suggestion — tune in design)

  • Base: near-black / deep charcoal (#0B0D12-ish) + off-white, for the premium Linear-dark feel. The product lives in dark mode comfortably.
  • Primary accent: a single sharp "equip" color — a forged steel-blue or electric cyan (the HUD glow), used sparingly for CTAs and the crest.
  • Crit accent (used rarely, with intent): one warm "critical-strike" highlight — a molten amber / forge-orange — reserved for moments that signify a crit or the First Strike unlock. Scarcity is what keeps it premium.
  • Rule: two accents, max. Restraint reads as expensive; a rainbow reads as a game-jam.

Type

  • One confident sans for headlines (geometric/grotesk), one clean readable sans for body. No decorative/fantasy fonts, ever. The HUD vocabulary supplies the flavor — the type stays sober.

Motifs that work (tasteful)

  • A HUD stat-readout treatment for the metric glossary (hit rate / crit / loadout / Fatigue Wall) — clean labels, monospace numerals, thin rules.
  • A loadout card-grid for the 4×24 (4 angles × 24 — a tidy slotted grid reads instantly as "a loadout").
  • A crest/sigil as the recurring brand stamp.
  • The "Forge" rendered as a clean iconographic anvil/spark mark — minimal, not literal blacksmith art.

What to AVOID (hard list)

  • WoW clipart, fantasy-game UI chrome, dragons, swords-as-clipart, glowing runes, pixel-art, parchment textures, faux-leather/metal bevels, lens-flare "EPIC" energy.
  • Loud gradients, rainbow palettes, more than two accent colors.
  • Emoji-as-design, exclamation-mark energy, hype-sticker badges.
  • Fake trust furniture: invented star ratings, logo walls, "as seen in" bars, fabricated testimonials (see §9 — this is a visual proof-only rule too).
  • Anything that makes a $20–100K/mo buyer feel he's looking at a game and not a weapon. The test: does it look like a tool a serious operator would equip, or a costume? Equip, never costume.
