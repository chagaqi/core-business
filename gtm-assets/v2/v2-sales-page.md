## [THE STAKES — name the bleed before the cure]

Here's what the wait actually costs you.

- **The same question, two hundred times.** Every long-lead presale turns your inbox into "any update?" on repeat, even when the timeline is already on the product page. People forget. Then they email.
- **Disputes that hit your processor.** Buyers open Stripe disputes saying you never shipped, three weeks into a ten-week lead time. Each one is a fee, a clawback, and a mark against your merchant account in the exact category card networks watch most closely: crowdfunded, long-delivery.
- **The "scam" word.** A buyer who can't get a calm, specific answer doesn't conclude that production takes time. She concludes she's been had, and she says so in the review the next hundred buyers read.
- **Support flying blind.** Your helpdesk is a strong generalist. It doesn't know which orders are preorders or what to say at day 12 versus day 89. Your agents click into Shopify, then the order, then the preorder app, just to guess at a ship date.

Presale isn't the mistake. It's the best cash-flow model in commerce. The wait is just the part nobody built a tool for.

## [THE PROBLEM NARRATIVE — the arc of a presale buyer]

Most CS tools treat "where's my order" as one question. It isn't. It's the same words meaning something different depending on how long the buyer has waited. The buyer changes, and the right answer changes with her.

### Day 7 — Curious.
The order is fresh and she's still excited. She checks for a tracking page that doesn't exist yet, sees nothing, and sends a friendly "just making sure my order went through?"

Easiest moment to win, and the easiest to fumble. "Your order is being processed" tells her nothing and trains her to ask again. Compare: "You're confirmed and on schedule. Your batch is in tooling now, ships in your weeks 9–11 window. I'll message you the moment it moves to production, so you won't have to ask." She stops checking. You just removed a week of future tickets in one reply.

### Day 30 — Restless.
A month in. The excitement's gone, the card statement showed up and the product didn't. She's quietly wondering whether she handed money to someone about to ghost her.

She might not email yet. But she's one bad day from a refund request, re-reading your campaign for a reason to bail. A specific update rebuilds the trust the silence eroded: "Honest update, we're in the second production run, here's a photo of where your unit sits, still on track for your window." A Klaviyo blast can't do this. It's one-way and she knows it. A reply that names her order and her timeline can. Day 30 is where the relationship is quietly won or lost, and almost nobody works it.

### Day 60 — Frustrated.
Two months. Now it's "This is taking forever. I want a refund." Here's the trap: founders read that as a decision. It usually isn't. At day 60, "I want a refund" is often "convince me to keep waiting."

A canned line or a slow reply confirms her fear that you've checked out. The right move acknowledges the wait ("Totally fair to feel that at the two-month mark"), shows concrete movement ("your unit cleared QC this week and is in the final packing run"), and offers a step ("want me to hold it for priority dispatch?"). That reply often turns the most frustrated buyer into the one who leaves the good review. It only works if your support knows the unit cleared QC. Reading the real timeline, not guessing.

### Day 89 — Dispute-risk.
The highest-stakes moment in the wait. "Where IS my order?? Last chance before I dispute." You have maybe one reply and 24 hours.

Speed and honesty are the only things that work. Acknowledge she's waited longer than she should have, give her the single most reassuring true fact you have ("your tracking is generating now, I'll send it within 24 hours"), and give her a reason to route the next move to you instead of her bank. A chargeback here is a fee, a product you've already built, and a customer who tells everyone. It's the highest-leverage message in your business, and the one your generic bot is least equipped to send. It answers a 90-day emergency with a 5-day shipping script.

Four moments, four buyers, one inbox. Every presale brand lives this on every order, hundreds at a time, all on different clocks. That's the problem Tideover exists to solve.

## [THE MECHANISM — why this is a specialist layer, not another helpdesk]

You don't need another helpdesk. You have one, or you have an inbox, which is the same job with worse tools. What you've never had is something that understands the wait. Three things separate Tideover from every generic AI support tool on the App Store.

**1. It reads each order's real production timeline.**
A generic bot answers "where's my order" with the same logic whether you ship in 2 days or 120. Tideover reads the actual order, knows it's a presale, and knows the stage: tooling, production, QC, packing, dispatch. The reply is anchored to that order's truth, expressed as an honest confidence-band window ("ships in weeks 9–11"), never an invented hard date that comes back to bite you.

**2. It answers the day, not just the question.**
The same question means something different across the wait. Curious at day 7 gets calm confirmation. Restless at day 30 gets proof of movement. Dispute-risk at day 89 gets the save. No generalist tool has this, because none were built around a 60–120 day emotional journey.

**3. It's a bolt-on. Presale tickets only.**
Tideover sits on top of the helpdesk you already run and routes only presale tickets to the layer. Every other ticket stays put, handled exactly as today. The incumbents are great generalists with a presale-shaped hole. This fills the hole and leaves the rest alone.

The part that matters most for trust: during the pilot, nothing is autonomous. Every reply is drafted for you, and you approve it before it reaches a customer. Automation comes later, and only for language we've watched work in a real inbox.

The moat isn't the AI. LLMs are a commodity. It's the day-by-day reassurance playbooks and the outcome data, built from real operator experience and real partner inboxes. That's why this starts as a concierge service, not a software download.
