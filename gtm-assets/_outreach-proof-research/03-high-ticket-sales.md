## 1. The spine: every framework is the same five moves

Strip the branding and AH, JM, CG, JN all teach one structure. They differ on tonality and emphasis, not bones:

1. **Frame the call** (set agenda, get permission, earn the right to ask).
2. **Discover the gap** (current state → desired state → the cost of the gap). This is 60–70% of the call. The prospect does most of the talking.
3. **Make them say the consequence out loud** (what happens if nothing changes). This creates the urgency. You don't supply it; you extract it.
4. **Present the solution as the bridge** to the gap they just described, in their own words. Short. Outcome, not mechanism.
5. **Reveal price after desire, then handle objections as questions, not fights.**

The whole game is *the prospect closes themselves*. CG: build the beliefs "so you don't have to convince the prospect to buy, the prospect closes themselves." JM: "buyers don't respond to pressure, they respond to clarity." AH: by the time price comes up "the prospect has already talked themselves into wanting the outcome." This is the through-line across all four operators and it is the single most important takeaway.

**2026 flag.** Only 26% of B2B reps hit quota in 2025 (vs a historical ~70% benchmark). Buyers are more skeptical and more researched. The pressure-close era is dead; the discovery-heavy, self-close model is what survives. This is *tailwind* for the proof-first Adwield motion, which already refuses to hard-close. The frameworks below are aligned with where selling is going, not against it.

## 2. Hormozi — C.L.O.S.E.R (AH)

The exact flow his portfolio sales teams ran. Six steps; if you skip steps "it won't work" (his words).

- **C — Clarify why they came.** "Clarify why the person is on the phone with you." Verbatim opener: *"What made you reach out specifically?"* / *"What are we trying to solve here?"* Even on a "just looking," dig: the outreach itself signals a problem. Get them to state the problem and the desired after-state in their own words.
- **L — Label the problem.** Name it back precisely so they feel heard and the problem becomes concrete. Verbatim: *"So if I'm hearing you correctly, it sounds like you've been working incredibly hard, but you feel like your revenue has hit a ceiling…"* They must acknowledge the problem exists before you can sell the fix.
- **O — Overview past pain (the "pain cycle").** *"Tell me a little about what you've done so far to try and get past this."* / *"What have you tried that hasn't worked? What happened when you tried that?"* Document what they liked/disliked about prior solutions. This is where you learn how to position so you're not the next thing that failed them.
- **S — Sell the vacation, not the plane flight.** *"Sell the vacation, not the plane, right?"* Sell the destination, never the journey. "The details sound like work." Verbatim future-pace: *"If we were able to completely solve this together, what would that mean for you?"* Outcomes only. The mechanism is a buzzkill.
- **E — Explain away concerns.** "Objections are questions in disguise." Never dismiss or steamroll. Acknowledge → validate → answer with logic. AH buckets objections into three: **environment/timing** ("the economy," "bad season"), **other people** ("need my partner"), **internal** ("not sure it's for me / I can't do it"). Identify the decision-maker; neutralize risk with guarantee + process clarity.
- **R — Reinforce the decision.** Added "years later" because "the sale continues throughout the entire customer relationship." People decide within 48 hrs of buying whether they'll buy again. Execute onboarding in the first 24 hrs, send progress touches before the next meeting. Kills buyer's remorse and refunds.

**Pricing reveal:** late, after S. By the time you state a number, the prospect has already verbalized the outcome's worth. AH doesn't give a verbatim price script in the public CLOSER material, but the *sequence* is the point: desire before number, every time.

**Fit for us:** CLOSER is the cleanest backbone and it maps 1:1 onto the existing `v1-sales-call-kit` call map (opener/frame → pain-dig → Track confirmation → pitch → guarantee → ask). The "O / pain cycle" is exactly our agency-burn pain-dig. "Sell the vacation" is why our pitch is "winners on a schedule before fatigue," not "24 AI variations." Adopt the spine, keep our proof-first voice.

## 3. Jeremy Miner — NEPQ (JM)

Neuro-Emotional Persuasion Questions. The signature: you almost never make a statement; you ask a question that makes the prospect argue *your* case to themselves. Tonality is half the method (curious, slightly detached, never eager). Seven question types in sequence:

1. **Connection questions** — disarm, shift "sales call" → "real conversation." Put focus entirely on them. Verbatim: *"Can you walk me through how you're currently handling [X]?"* / *"What attracted your attention?"* / *"Have you found what you're looking for?"*
2. **Situation questions** — surface facts. *"What are you using now…?"* / *"What are you doing now for…?"* Keep these few; they bore the prospect if overdone.
3. **Problem awareness questions** — the emotional door. What's the problem, why do they have it, how is it affecting them. *"Do you like what your [current solution] is doing?"* / *"Why don't you like…?"* / *"What happens when that process breaks down, who does it affect the most?"*
4. **Solution awareness questions** — what have they tried, what worked/didn't, what would they want instead. *"What have you done to try to change this?"* / *"What criteria would you use if you were going to work with someone else?"* This positions you as the trusted authority, not a pitcher.
5. **Consequence questions** — the urgency engine. Make them picture the cost of inaction, out loud. *"Have you thought about what would happen if you don't do anything about this, [name]?"* / *"What do you think will happen if this stays the same over the next 6 months?"* / the sighed version: *"(Sigh) I mean, what if you don't do anything and your [problem] keeps getting worse the next 3, 6, even 12 months?"*
6. **Qualifying questions** — confirm it matters enough to act. *"How important is it for you to [fix this]?"* / *"Why is that important to you now?"*
7. **Transition / commitment** — neutral, low-pressure handoff to the pitch. *"Would it be appropriate to talk again to see if we could help you?"* / *"How do you want to proceed from here?"*

**NEPQ objection handling — the 3-step formula (Clarify → Discuss → Diffuse).** This is the most-cited, best-corroborated piece of NEPQ and it's directly liftable:

- **Clarify.** Never answer an objection; question it first. Verbatim: *"When you say [repeat their words], what do you mean by that?"* / *"Why do you ask that?"* / *"How did you arrive at thinking that way?"* / *"Can I ask where you got that information?"* Price example: prospect says "it's too expensive," you say *"How do you mean, too expensive?"*
- **Discuss.** Talk it through like a friend, conversationally. Ask *how they'd see themselves resolving it.* You're not defending; you're co-solving.
- **Diffuse.** Lead them to dissolve it in their own mind. *"Suppose it wasn't what you thought it was?"* / *"What if there was a way you could [outcome], would that help you?"* / *"If we could solve [the thing they're worried about], would that change things?"*

**Old-model vs new-model (JM's core thesis):** old = pitch harder, push faster, robotic scripts, "always be closing." New = pull the prospect in with curiosity and emotional intelligence; "sound human without sounding desperate." This is the 2026-correct stance.

**Fit for us:** NEPQ's *question discipline* is the upgrade to our pain-dig. Our current kit asks good questions but states a lot; NEPQ says shut up and let the question do the work. **Consequence questions are the missing piece** in our call kit (we surface buying temperature but don't make them voice the cost of the dying winner). And the **Clarify-Discuss-Diffuse** formula is a clean operating system to bolt onto every objection in our §2 bank: clarify the real objection before deploying the scripted answer. *Caveat:* NEPQ taken neat can feel manipulative/therapist-y if over-leaned. Use the structure, dial the "(sigh)" theatrics way down for a skeptical operator audience.

## 4. Cole Gordon — the Belief Ladder (CG)

The most useful *diagnostic*. CG says a prospect buys only when 7 beliefs are all true. Your job on the call is to build the missing ones with questions, in order. Miss one, the deal dies there.

The 7 beliefs (verbatim from his framework):
1. **Pain / unfulfilled desire** — "No problem. No sale."
2. **Doubt** — they believe they can't fix it themselves (or not as fast/well as with you).
3. **Cost** — they believe the cost of inaction exceeds the investment.
4. **Desire** — they believe fixing it materially improves their life/business.
5. **Money** — they have the resources, and (CG splits this) the **willingness** to allocate that budget to *your* solution specifically.
6. **Support** — stakeholders back solving the problem (partner, team, spouse).
7. **Trust** — in you, in the *method*, and in the process to deliver. "Trust in the methodology is even more important" than trust in the company.

**Call structure (CG):** introduction → information gathering → transition → pitch → **commit phase** (get them to buy into the thesis you just presented, *before* price) → objections. The commit phase is distinctive: you get a yes on the *logic* before you ever quote.

**On the money objection (verbatim):** *"Money is very easy to handle if they one hundred percent believe this is what they need to get where they're trying to go."* Translation: a price objection is almost always an unbuilt belief upstream (usually #2 doubt, #3 cost-of-inaction, or #7 trust). Don't fight the number; go back and build the missing belief.

**Fit for us:** Use the Belief Ladder as a **pre-close checklist**. Before quoting a retainer, silently confirm: do they believe their winner is dying (pain), that they can't out-produce it solo (doubt), that the dead winner costs more than $2.5K/mo (cost), that fresh winners change their P&L (desire), that they have spend to redirect (money), that the media buyer is bought-in not threatened (support, see objection #17), and that the made-for-them sample earned trust in the *method* (trust). Any "no" = the real objection, regardless of what they say out loud. This directly explains our existing buying-temperature read: a "5–7" means a belief is unbuilt; find which one.

## 5. Josh Nelson — the agency close system (JN) — HIGHEST FIT

JN is the only source who built and sold the *exact thing we sell* (a marketing agency to local/SMB owners) at size. His system is plainer than the others and reports a hard number.

**Call structure (verbatim sequence):**
1. Build rapport.
2. **Set the agenda AND ask for a decision by end of call.** Front-load the close so "I need to think about it" is pre-empted. ("By the end of today we'll both know if this is a fit, and if it is, I'll ask you to make a decision. Fair?")
3. Discovery: goals, challenges, timeline.
4. **Gap analysis** — the pivot: *"Can I show you where your current strategy is falling short?"*
5. Present offer *with pricing, deliverables, and client examples* together.
6. Close: *"Let's schedule your kickoff call. Does Thursday at 3 work?"* (assumptive, action-based, not "do you want to buy").
7. Collect payment, send agreement.

**Objection system — 3 steps:** Acknowledge → Isolate the real objection → Handle with confidence.
- **"I need to think about it":** *"I totally understand, I like to think about things too. What would you be thinking about most: price, results, or value?"* → answer it → *"Did that answer your concern? Should we move forward?"*
- **"I need to talk to my partner":** *"Absolutely, I get that. What would your partner be saying no to: price, service, or risk?"* (isolates the hidden real objection, same move as NEPQ Clarify and CG's belief diagnosis).

**The number: 41% close rate.** JN states "at my agency, we average 41%," attributed to running this objection system "without pressure or manipulation." **Single-source, self-reported, no sample size/timeframe disclosed.** Treat 41% as an aspirational ceiling for a warm, qualified agency call, not a benchmark to plan revenue against. The *believable* shape from the broader data: a well-qualified high-ticket close call converts ~20–40%; cold/unqualified far lower.

**Fit for us:** JN's structure is the closest template to our reality. Two liftable upgrades: (a) **ask for the decision in the agenda** ("by the end we'll both know, and I'll ask you to decide") — our kit currently doesn't pre-frame the decision, and this is the cheapest fix for ghosting; (b) the **"price, results, or value?" isolation question** is a one-line objection-decoder we can paste straight in.

## 6. Pricing-reveal timing — the consensus

All four operators agree, and it's the highest-confidence finding (corroborated 4 ways):

- **Never quote before desire is built and the consequence is voiced.** Price stated into a vacuum anchors against nothing and triggers a reflexive "too expensive." (This is already locked in our copy standard's warm-reply tree: "a number now anchors against nothing.")
- **Sequence: gap → consequence → outcome/vacation → micro-commit on the logic → THEN price.** CG's "commit phase" and AH's CLOSER both put a yes-on-the-thesis *before* the number.
- **Anchor against their own P&L, never a competitor's rate card** (already in our §3.2: "$2.5K out of a single profitable day of ad spend"). AH's "sell the vacation" is the same instinct: frame the number against the outcome's value, not against other vendors.
- **A price objection is a belief gap, not a money problem** (CG verbatim; JM's Clarify step; JN's isolation question all converge here). Don't discount. Go back and find the unbuilt belief. This validates our locked "never discount the retainer to close" rule and the downsell-ladder-not-discount choreography in §3.4.

**2026 nuance:** with buyers more researched, the deck-send / "let me get pricing over to you" move is weaker than ever. Price lives on the call, after value, or the deal leaks into the inbox and dies. Our artifact-first model (they've already seen the made-for-them ad before the call) front-loads the desire-building, which means we arrive at the price reveal warmer than a cold pitch would. That's a structural edge worth protecting.

## 7. Discovery framework — the synthesized best-of for our call

Merge of CLOSER's spine, NEPQ's question discipline, CG's belief check, JN's decision-frame. Maps onto the existing `v1-sales-call-kit` call map:

1. **Frame (JN):** agenda + "by the end we'll both know if it's a fit, and if it is I'll ask you to decide." Pre-empts the think-about-it.
2. **Clarify (AH):** "What made you take this call?" Let them open. Their unprompted framing is the best Track signal (already in our kit).
3. **Situation, then problem-awareness (JM):** spend tier, current creative throughput, then *"Do you like what your current creative is doing for you?"* / *"What happens when the winner dies, who feels it most?"* Ask, then shut up.
4. **Overview the pain cycle (AH/CLOSER-O):** "What have you tried that hasn't worked?" (agency burn, freelancers, in-house). This is our existing agency-burn dig.
5. **Consequence (JM) — the upgrade we're missing:** *"What happens to the account if the current winner dies in 6 weeks and the next one isn't ready?"* Make them voice the cost. Don't supply it.
6. **Label + Track confirmation (AH/our kit):** say the problem back, lock the route (Refuge vs Leverage). Micro-commit.
7. **Belief check (CG) — silent pre-close:** before quoting, confirm pain/doubt/cost/desire/money/support/trust are all built. Any gap = the real objection.
8. **Sell the vacation (AH):** "winners on a schedule before fatigue kills the one you've got." Outcome, not 24-variations mechanism.
9. **Guarantee + cap (our kit):** risk reversal *is* the lever.
10. **Commit on the logic (CG), then price (all):** "Does it make sense to move forward?" → yes on thesis → then the sized retainer.
11. **Objections (JM 3-step + JN isolation):** Clarify the real one ("when you say X, what do you mean?" / "is it price, results, or risk?") → Discuss → Diffuse → deploy the scripted §2 answer.
12. **Close (JN):** assumptive next-action ("want me to send the Stripe invoice and the intake?"), with our locked non-pushy out.
13. **Reinforce (AH-R):** onboard inside 24 hrs, send progress before the first cycle reads. Kills remorse, protects renewal.

**Flagged / single-source-weak:**
- **JN's 41% close rate** — single-source, self-reported, no sample size. Use as ceiling, not benchmark.
- **SetSmart's "9.7x more likely to book" (from 828K AI conversations)** — one aggregator source, about *setting* not closing. Interesting, not load-bearing.
- Exact NEPQ "(sigh)" tonality theatrics — real in his material but risky for a skeptical-operator audience; structure yes, theater no.
- AH's "people decide within 48 hrs whether they'll buy again" — stated as fact in CLOSER material, no cited study. Directionally sound (post-purchase dissonance is real), treat the number as rule-of-thumb.

**Where 2026 differs from the older advice:** the foundational frameworks (CLOSER ~2021, NEPQ, CG's ladder) all predate the current buyer-skepticism collapse but *point the right direction* (discovery-heavy, low-pressure, self-close). The dated parts are tactical: aggressive urgency/scarcity, "always be closing," deck-then-price. The aggregators (SetSmart) add the genuinely new layer: AI setters now do qualification at a fraction of human cost, but **the AI does not replace the closer** on a high-ticket call. For a solo founder, that means: automate setting/qualification (Gooseworks, our prospecting rig), keep the human on the close. The proof-first, no-hard-close Adwield motion is already aligned with the 2026 model. The frameworks here sharpen the *discovery and objection discipline* inside a call structure we've largely already built.
