## 1. Reply-handler — prospect says "not interested / not right now"
**Operator:** Josh Braun
**Link:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/josh-braun_josh-a-prospect-responded-to-my-cold-email-activity-7074138982707556352-T9Cm
**Proof:** Josh Braun Sales Training founder. Post: 156 reactions, 5 comments.
**Type:** email reply to a soft "no." **Credibility: top-tier (own script, real engagement).**

> Jim - It seems like you have a lot on your plate and need to focus on what matters most. Here's my ask. (And I know it's a big ask because you don't owe me anything) We create educational content for Directors of Rev Ops about trends affecting sales compensation. Here's an article on how Albert Wong, Director of Rev Ops at Gong, uses SPIIFS to motivate reps. Would it be alright if I add you to our email list? You can unsubscribe anytime. Either way, I understand you're extremely busy, so thank you for responding.

**Why it works (my note):** drops the meeting-ask. Trades it for one piece of useful content and permission to stay on the list. The "big ask, you don't owe me anything" line removes the pressure that triggers a hard no.

## 2. Reply-handler — "send me an email" (on a cold call)
**Operator:** Josh Braun
**Links:** https://joshbraun.com/send-me-an-email/ and https://joshbraun.com/how-to-defuse-send-me-a-email/
**Proof:** Josh Braun Sales Training; technique credited to Chris Voss's mirroring.
**Type:** live call reply to the brush-off. **Credibility: top-tier.**

First response:
> To make sure I don't do a disservice by sending you an email that's not relevant, would it be okay if I asked you a couple of quick questions?

If they still deflect ("just send me anything"):
> I might be misreading here, but typically when people ask me to send them anything, it's just a nice way of saying they're not interested, which is not a problem at all. I know you didn't ask me to call you. Is that the case here?

**Why it works (my note):** names the polite-brush-off out loud so the prospect either re-engages or frees you. Reusable on chat/DM replies, not just calls.

## 3. Three follow-ups that replace "just checking in"
**Operator:** Josh Braun
**Link:** https://joshbraun.com/how-to-write-follow-up-emails/
**Proof:** Josh Braun Sales Training. He explicitly lists the dead openers to avoid ("Just checking in," "Bumping this to the top in your inbox," "Did you get the email below?," "Just following up") — same kill-list as our copy-standard.
**Type:** bump/value follow-ups. **Credibility: top-tier (own swipe).**

Value-bump (data hook):
> John, we analyzed 937 cold calls that booked meetings. And have a slideshow on what each of those calls has in common. Is this something you'd like to see?

Genuine-compliment bump (referencing their content):
> John, I enjoyed your two cents on building a winning sales culture. It was interesting to hear your thoughts on making time for coaching and why it matters. You rock.

Relevant-resource bump:
> John, thought you'd enjoy this HBR article on building a productive sales culture. In particular the bit about 'firing' customers that, despite all attempts, remain unprofitable accounts.

**Why it works (my note):** every bump is a deposit (data, praise, or a resource), never a withdrawal. None mention the prior email. Maps directly onto our "no dead openers" rule.

## 4. The shame-free re-write of a ghost follow-up
**Operator:** Josh Braun
**Link:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/josh-braun_heres-a-cold-email-follow-up-message-i-received-activity-7085234104606019584--Egj
**Proof:** Josh Braun. Post: 466 likes, 75 comments.
**Type:** ghost re-engage. **Credibility: top-tier (high engagement, skeptical commenters debating it).**

The bad email he received (verbatim, as the foil):
> I haven't heard back from you. Do you have any questions? I think you're wasting your potential.

His re-write:
> Hey Josh - I know you're crazy busy. There are lots of people competing for your time and resources. Plus, on top of that, you have the Saleshacker webinar coming up. Here's my ask (and I know it's a big ask because you don't owe me a response.) Would you like to hit the pause button on expanding reach on TikTok? Either way, keep up the great work on the podcast. The 2 minute episodes are so easy to digest.

**Why it works (my note):** zero guilt-trip. Acknowledges a real, specific thing on the prospect's plate (the webinar, the podcast), then a low-friction one-line ask. The "you don't owe me a response" defuses the obligation that makes people ghost harder. Same shape as our Adwield/Tideover "no chase" re-engage.

## 5. Sam Nelson's Agoge Sequence — bumps + 3 breakup emails (full verbatim)
**Operator:** Sam Nelson (built the SDR org at Outreach.io)
**Link (free PDF):** https://blog.contactout.io/wp-content/uploads/2023/10/30MPC-x-Sam-Nelsons-Agoge-Sequence.pdf (published via 30MPC)
**Proof:** "Sam - founder at SDRLeader.com and Agoge and blue-haired sales wizard" (from the doc); ran SDRs at Outreach. 15-touch sequence over 27 days.
**Type:** reply-bumps + breakup emails. **Credibility: top-tier (named operator, free, widely circulated).**
Note: templates are written for Outreach.io's own product; swap the product name.

**The one-line reply-bumps (Step 4 — reply so the original email stays visible):**
> "Any thoughts?"
> "Just seeing if you got this..."
> "What do you think?"

**Mid-sequence bump (Steps 7 / 12 — slightly longer, original email still visible):**
> Hi {{first_name}},
> Making sure my last note reached your inbox. I know things can get lost and I wanted to get back on your radar.
> When can I get a few minutes on your calendar to talk?
> Thanks,
> {{sender.first_name}}

**The "wrong contact?" bump (Step 11 — contrite, asks for a referral):**
> Hey {{first_name}},
> I want to be absolutely sure I have the right contact. If I missed the mark here, I apologize.
> If you've been busy, but are the appropriate person, could we put a few minutes on the calendar to discuss Outreach in the near future?
> If not, I'd appreciate your passing me along.
> Thanks in advance,
> {{sender.first_name}}

**Breakup email #1 — "the new rep, what went wrong?" (Step 15):**
> Subject: is {{work_phone}} the best number for you?
> Hey {{first_name}},
> I'm pretty new here and I'm trying to figure out why I haven't been able to get a hold of you. {{account.name}} was identified as a company that would get an extremely high ROI from Outreach and I'm trying to find out where I went wrong.
> We're both in sales here and I'm sure you've been in a similar situation where you've done everything possible and still can't get the attention of a high-priority prospect.
> For my own growth as a salesperson, would you be so kind as to just tell me why you have not responded?
> Thanks for any help,
> {{sender.first_name}}

**Breakup email #2 — "one-way conversation" (Step 15 alt):**
> Subject: :(
> {{first_name}}
> I don't want to be rude but I have been reaching out for weeks and this is starting to feel like a one-way conversation.
> I've researched you and your company, I've called you 6 times, I've left messages, InMails... everything in my power. I KNOW Outreach can be a game changer for {{account.name}}.
> Even if you don't have time for a demo, can we at least chat so you can see why I'm being so persistent?
> {{sender.first_name}}

**Breakup email #3 — "should I go kick rocks?" (Step 15 alt):**
> Subject: should I go kick rocks?
> {{first_name}},
> I've been reaching out to you for quite some time and have yet to hear back. I'm sure you're either:
> 1. inundated with projects
> 2. out of town on travel
> 3. stuck under a giant boulder
> If it's #3, let me know so I can send help! To recap, these are the issues we solve:
> 1. missing revenue goals
> 2. failure to follow up on leads effectively
> 3. disparity in rep production
> If any of these resonate with you, we should connect! When would be most ideal for your schedule?
> Best,
> {{sender.first_name}}

**Nelson's own principle (verbatim from the doc):**
> You're not really breaking up; you're trying out a different approach one last time... it's better to be remembered than liked. We want a strong reaction (preferably a positive one, but we'll take what we can get).

**Why it works (my note):** the strongest breakup of the three for our voice is #1 — the humble "help me learn why" ask gets replies because it costs the prospect nothing and flatters them as the expert. #2 leans on persistence (risky, can read as needy). #3 uses humor to earn the strong reaction. Note the bumps are pure inbox-resurfacing, not new pitches — that's the mechanic, not the copy, worth stealing.

## 6. The "new email" re-engage with a customer proof point

> Subject: did you get my voicemail?

> [Quoted customer name] at [account name] said, "insert quote here."

## Cross-cutting patterns (what the proven operators agree on)

1. **No dead openers — universally.** Braun and Nelson both explicitly ban "just checking in / following up / bumping this." Confirms our copy-standard kill-list is the operator consensus, not a quirk.
2. **The bump's job is to resurface, not re-pitch.** Nelson's one-liners reply on the original thread so the first email stays visible. Mechanic > copy.
3. **Breakups get replies because they lower the ask, not raise it.** The best (Braun #1, Nelson breakup #1) ask the prospect to *teach you why they didn't reply* — flattering, zero-commitment.
4. **Remove obligation to remove the ghost.** "You don't owe me a response / anything" appears in both of Braun's best scripts. Counterintuitive, but it's what unsticks the reply.
5. **One piece of fresh value per touch.** Data, a customer quote, a resource, or a specific observation — never a bare "any update?"
