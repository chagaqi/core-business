### Issue #5 — "Hold-rate is the only metric you control"
**Subject:** the one ad metric that's actually yours
**Preview:** ROAS depends on 9 things you don't own. This depends on one you do.

- **Lead.** ROAS moves with price, offer, landing page, audience, seasonality, and the auction's mood. Hold-rate (3-second hold / thumbstop) is almost entirely creative-controllable. The one performance number with your fingerprints on it. *(Audit note: the page leans hold-rate hard. Frame it as the metric I'd build around and ask whether it's the one they'd actually buy on, not one they already trust.)*
- **Body 1 — why hold-rate leads.** It softens before ROAS craters. Watching ROAS to time a refresh is watching the lagging indicator.
- **Body 2 — the 3 things that move hold-rate, in order.** The first frame (problem, not logo). The first spoken line (specific, not "hey guys"). Caption pacing on the first 3 seconds. None require budget. They're craft.
- **Body 3 — the test.** Put a fatigue clock on your top ad, log its 3-second hold weekly. The day the line bends, the replacement should already be in testing. `[CASE STUDY PLACEHOLDER]`
- **Close + soft CTA.** "Track this yourself starting today. If the gap is having the hands to produce replacements at cadence, that's what I fill. Reply HOLD for the tracking sheet."
- **Footer.** Hard cap of 5 brands, functional consumables only. The notes are free and stay free.

### Issue #6 — "The 4 angles I'd test on almost any functional-consumables SKU"
**Subject:** four reasons people actually buy (and why you're only testing one)
**Preview:** Most brands test variations of a single reason-to-buy. Here are four.

- **Lead.** The angle/variation confusion costs more than any targeting mistake. Define both in two sentences: angle = why, variation = which execution.
- **Body — the 4 reusable angle families** (SKU-agnostic skeleton):
  1. **The replacement** — swap the thing you already do (the 3pm coffee, the soda, the juice bar). Lowest-friction opener in this category.
  2. **The objection-killer** — lead with the top 3-star-review complaint and dismantle it.
  3. **The price math** — per-serving cost vs the alternative they already buy. Strong for considered-purchase AOVs.
  4. **The ritual** — anchor the product to a specific moment in the day. Builds the habit that builds LTV.
- **Body 2 — how to pick which leads.** Off the AOV and the top objection, not off taste. Worked example contrasting a $90 SKU and a $35 SKU.
- **Close + soft CTA.** "Pick the 3 or 4 that fit your SKU and you've got a cycle of tests. Reply PACK for the 10-script template these expand into."
- **Footer.** 5-brand cap line.

### Issue #7 — "The claim that gets your account banned (and how to rewrite it)"
**Subject:** the line that kills supplement accounts
**Preview:** It's not a bad ad that gets you pulled. It's one sentence.

- **Lead.** For functional consumables the existential risk isn't a low-performing ad. It's a claim you can't substantiate. The FTC and Meta act on language. One sentence takes down an account.
- **Body 1 — the 3 claim tiers, with examples** per category (greens / nootropic / beverage / mushroom): safe structure-function ("supports," "helps maintain"); risky (specific effect claims, "boosts," "clinically" with no study on file); account-ending (disease claims, implied intoxication, "cures").
- **Body 2 — the rewrite move.** Keep the persuasion, drop the flagged language. Three before/after rewrites that preserve the sell.
- **Body 3 — the gate as a process.** Every script screened before render. Why a brand-blind vendor skips this and why that's a liability you inherit.
- **Close + soft CTA.** "Paste a claim you're unsure about and I'll tell you which side of the line it's on. Or reply CLAIMS for the cheat sheet."
- **Footer.** 5-brand cap. This is the gate every script of mine passes anyway.
