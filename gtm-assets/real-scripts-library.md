## How to read this

**What maps to our own scripts.** Our Adwield and Tideover copy in `copy-standard.md` isn't a new theory. It's the same playbook these operators run, with the hype stripped and one upgrade.

- **Our "no calendar link cold" rule** = Welsh, Ruiz, McKenna. None drop a booking link in a first touch.
- **Our warm-reply objection tree (ask, dig, dig, soft ask)** = Marcos Ruiz's $3M DM flow, almost beat for beat.
- **Our "defer the price to value, never quote blind"** = Miner's Commitment questions and the Prospeo "I'll think about it" rebuttal. Three sources land on it.
- **Our "no dead openers" kill-list** = Josh Braun and Sam Nelson both ban "just checking in / following up / bumping this" by name. Operator consensus, not our quirk.
- **Our re-engage ("no chase, one useful thing")** = Braun's ghost re-write and Nelson's breakup #1. Lower the ask, remove the obligation.
- **Our artifact-first move (send the built ad, not "some ideas")** = the upgrade. Every agency operator below opens with a claim ("I have some ideas," "I help businesses fix that"). Adwield opens with the finished ad. Same move-2 in their frameworks, stronger version.

**The 2-3 patterns that repeat across all the real scripts:**

1. **One specific researched observation, then one named result.** Not three adjectives. A funding round (Welsh), a hiring post (Lavender), a competitor's review count (Prospeo), a named client win (Berman, Nelson). The proof is concrete or it isn't there.
2. **The ask is a question about interest, not a meeting demand.** "Would you be interested in speaking?" (Abraham). "Worth a quick chat?" (HubSpot). "Are you interested in...?" (Nemo). Lower the friction of the first reply.
3. **On a stall, clarify it. Don't fight it.** Miner, Prospeo, and Braun all answer "think about it / send me an email" by naming the real objection out loud instead of pushing. Removing the obligation is what unsticks the reply.

**Source-quality marks used below:** `[VERBATIM]` operator's literal published words. `[WEAK]` vendor library or self-reported number, usable but not a proven operator's own call. `[PARAPHRASE]` reconstruction, exact wording not confirmed; kept out of the quote where possible and never sold as verbatim.

### 1. Daniel Fazio — 3-email sequence `[VERBATIM]`

> Subject: Quick question about @ [[InstagramUsername]]
>
> Hey [[First Name]]. Saw you popping up on my Instagram Explore Page. You're killin it! [[Or some other variation of a compliment that you've done personal research on them]]

>

> Subject: Re: Quick question about @ [[InstagramUsername]]
>

> Subject: Re: Quick question about @ [[InstagramUsername]]

## Sales Call

### Discovery

### 1. Jeremy Miner (NEPQ) — discovery questions `[VERBATIM]`

Problem Awareness:
> Can you walk me through the biggest challenges you're dealing with right now?
> What's happening in your business that made you start looking for help?
> How long has this been going on?
> What's been the impact on your day-to-day or your team?

Consequence:
> What happens if nothing changes in the next 6-12 months?
> What would it mean for your business if this problem continues?
> What's the worst-case scenario if this goes unresolved?

Commitment:
> What would you need to see to feel confident moving forward?
> If we could solve [problem], would that be worth it for you?

His best-practice rule, verbatim:
> Listen 80%, talk 20%. Let the questions do the selling.

Source: Jeremy Miner (founder/CEO, 7th Level) — https://irp.cdn-website.com/3b74b76a/files/uploaded/NEPQ+SALES+SECRETS+CHEAT+SHEET.pdf (hub: https://7thlevelhq.com/nepq-cheat-sheet2/) — straight from his company's own free cheat-sheet PDF. ## Follow-up + Reply-handling

### 1. Josh Braun — "send me an email" defuse `[VERBATIM]`

First response:
> To make sure I don't do a disservice by sending you an email that's not relevant, would it be okay if I asked you a couple of quick questions?

If they still deflect:
> I might be misreading here, but typically when people ask me to send them anything, it's just a nice way of saying they're not interested, which is not a problem at all. I know you didn't ask me to call you. Is that the case here?

Source: Josh Braun (founder, Josh Braun Sales Training; ex-Jellyvision sales-training co-founder) — https://joshbraun.com/send-me-an-email/ (and https://joshbraun.com/how-to-defuse-send-me-a-email/) — runs the 100k+ "Badass B2B Growth Guide" list. Names the polite brush-off out loud so the prospect re-engages or frees you. Works on DM/chat replies, not just calls.

### 2. Josh Braun — "not interested / not right now" reply `[VERBATIM]`

> Jim - It seems like you have a lot on your plate and need to focus on what matters most. Here's my ask. (And I know it's a big ask because you don't owe me anything) We create educational content for Directors of Rev Ops about trends affecting sales compensation. Here's an article on how Albert Wong, Director of Rev Ops at Gong, uses SPIIFS to motivate reps. Would it be alright if I add you to our email list? You can unsubscribe anytime. Either way, I understand you're extremely busy, so thank you for responding.

Source: Josh Braun — https://www.linkedin.com/posts/josh-braun_josh-a-prospect-responded-to-my-cold-email-activity-7074138982707556352-T9Cm — post shows 156 reactions, 5 comments. Drops the meeting-ask, trades it for one useful article and list permission. "You don't owe me anything" removes the pressure that triggers a hard no.

### 3. Josh Braun — three follow-ups that replace "just checking in" `[VERBATIM]`

Value-bump (data hook):
> John, we analyzed 937 cold calls that booked meetings. And have a slideshow on what each of those calls has in common. Is this something you'd like to see?

Genuine-compliment bump:
> John, I enjoyed your two cents on building a winning sales culture. It was interesting to hear your thoughts on making time for coaching and why it matters. You rock.

Relevant-resource bump:
> John, thought you'd enjoy this HBR article on building a productive sales culture. In particular the bit about 'firing' customers that, despite all attempts, remain unprofitable accounts.

Source: Josh Braun — https://joshbraun.com/how-to-write-follow-up-emails/ — he explicitly bans the same dead openers our copy-standard does. Every bump is a deposit (data, praise, resource), never a bare "any update?", and none mention the prior email.

## Agency-specific

### 1. Nick Abraham — Leadbird "if I could / risk minimizer" formula `[VERBATIM]`

> The worst cold email pitch of all time: (I don't know how people still use this) "Are you taking on more clients?" It just sounds so scammy. Any client worth having wouldn't respond. Qualifying the leads would be a nightmare. You're much better off strictly focusing on providing value. Instead of baiting them into saying yes, say: If I could {generate X result} {risk minimizer} would you be interested in speaking? For example, for my lead generation agency, the script would say: If I could generate meeting-ready leads on a performance basis, would you be interested in speaking? Sending emails that bait prospects into replying wastes your time and theirs.

The template isolated:
> If I could {generate X result} {risk minimizer} would you be interested in speaking?

His filled-in version:
> If I could generate meeting-ready leads on a performance basis, would you be interested in speaking?

Source: Nick Abraham (founder, Leadbird) — https://www.linkedin.com/posts/nick-abraham_coldemail-prospecting-b2bsales-activity-7119728772051832833-dVJm — Leadbird "sends 1 to 1.5 million cold emails per month" with "over 150 active clients," positioned ~$10M lead-gen agency. Highest-signal entry here. The `{risk minimizer}` slot ("on a performance basis") is the load-bearing move, the same thing Adwield does by leading with a built artifact instead of a promise.

## What this proves

Every credible agency operator lands on the same four moves: one specific researched observation, one named result, the agency does the work, one ~15-min ask, no deck. The only difference is how much hype they pad around it. Our `copy-standard.md` strips the hype and makes one upgrade: where they all open with a claim ("I have some ideas," "I help businesses fix that"), Adwield opens with the finished ad already built. The artifact replaces the claim. That's a stronger version of their move-2, and none of these operators do it. Tideover's free teardown is the same move for presale support. The convergence is the point: we're not inventing, we're running the proven play one notch sharper.
