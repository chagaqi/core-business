# Agency Lead-Gen + Sales Conversations — sourced frameworks

Mined for Adwield (AI-native performance-ads agency selling DTC operators). Two sources:

- **DE** = Dan Englander, *The Agency Lead Generation Course 2.0* (Sales Schema: 50+ agencies, 7,000+ campaigns, 3,000+ booked meetings). The spine of this brief. Built for agencies selling 5–7 figure services to B2B. Maps almost 1:1 to our outbound-to-DTC-operators motion.
- **MW** = Maria Wendt, *Automation Sales Unlocked*. Instagram comment-to-DM / ManyChat funnel for selling courses and products. **Mostly off-target** for B2B cold outbound. A few transferable mechanics are flagged at the end; the rest is out of scope.

Benchmarks are from a referral-heavy, high-ticket agency context (2019–2020). Deliverability tactics have aged; mindset, copy, and qualification logic are timeless. Flags below.

---

## 1. Foundation / mindset (DE)

**Five Prospecting Commandments** — the operating mindset, not tactics:
1. **Habits over outcomes.** Become "the type of agency that prospects every day." KPIs help alignment but activity is what you control. Timeless.
2. **Prospecting is never optional.** Every excuse (want a big client, hiring, referrals, bad economy, too busy) is real but "can never stand in the way of if you talk to your market." Enterprise reps prospect regardless; agency owners treat it as optional and fall apart when busy. Timeless.
3. **Think probabilistically.** Uncertainty never leaves B2B. Decide on ~70% of the info you wish you had (Bezos). Below that = slow. All copy has a shelf life of weeks/months; staying ahead = constant testing, not a set-and-forget system. He's openly hostile to gurus selling "certainty." Timeless.
4. **Embrace the long game.** Outbound prospects are problem-aware, not solution-aware (unlike referrals/RFPs). Cycle is longer. First meeting = a brainstorm or "de-risk" call, never a close. Upside: control + scale, "never go hungry again." Timeless.
5. **Choose carefully at the buffet.** Ignore most new tactics/software. Core is permanent: "the right words to the right people." If a tactic doesn't fit your voice, toss it. Timeless.

**Reframe — "Sell, teach, or learn."** Every outreach is one of three. "If you can't sell, teach; if you can't teach, learn." Removes the ick of "putting someone in a process." Do it daily; meetings flow. Timeless.

**WHY it matters for us:** Chaga is solo/small. The failure mode DE names most ("we sell ourselves okay, then we get busy and it falls apart") is the exact risk in a 90-day sprint. Consistency is the deliverable.

## 2. The three hats (delegation model, works solo) (DE)

Don't think job titles. Think three task-buckets, worn by one person or split:

- **Strategy hat** — writes copy, builds targeting, owns the message. Needs market depth + creativity. **The person on the first call must approve the strategy** so there's no disconnect between what gets someone to reply and what's said on the call. Front-load to your best energy hours (mornings). Cadence: set at outset, then optimize **every 30 days** in small moves. Don't change lanes weekly; wait for **70% certainty** unless the campaign is clearly dead.
- **Ops hat** — list building, data hygiene, software setup, deliverability, do-not-contact lists. Systems thinker, moderate tech comfort, no coding. Acts as a **gatekeeper** who can veto a strategist's idea on data/deliverability grounds.
- **Hustle hat** — proactively works warm leads to booked calls; persistence by phone/email. Triggered by interest signals (opens, clicks, "follow up in a week"). Scheduled *after* signals arrive, not upfront. Cold calling doesn't work in this space; hustle on warm does.

**Scheduling:** uninterrupted blocks. Morning strategy → ops → hustle later. Strategy never gets pushed to end-of-day when you're wiped. Timeless; directly applicable solo.

## 3. Targeting / list build — the WHO comes first (DE)

**Everything starts with the prospect.** Before tech, copy, channels, CRM: define the target. Start with prospects most similar to your **best past clients** (strongest case studies, right terms of art). Even if you think you're industry-agnostic, prospects describe their problems in industry-specific language, so be industry-focused for the first campaign. Non-negotiable.

**Flashlight vs. Laser Pointer:**
- **Flashlight** = high volume (hundreds–200/wk), low personalization (first name + company only). "Purple/black belt play." Only works after years of dialed-in messaging. Not for beginners.
- **Laser pointer** = low volume (a few dozen–50/wk), high personalization, better deliverability, far higher reply + meeting rate. **Start here.**

**LinkedIn Sales Navigator build (the core research engine):**
- LinkedIn is where most data vendors scrape from; start at the source.
- **Filters:** Industry (use how the company describes *itself* — google it; e.g. Dell = "Information Technology and Services"). Geography. **Company headcount** (most accurate field; set the threshold to "smallest company you'd be happy to work with," e.g. 500+, then chase white whales later). **Title** (use actual title text, not the "function" field which LinkedIn guesses; titles are treated as exact, not Boolean, so add multiple title entries). **Seniority** (going *up* the food chain often converts easier than down).
- **Keyword field supports Boolean (AND/OR); the title field does not.**
- **80% rule:** scan profiles for ~80% fit, not perfection. Too broad → tighten; too narrow → loosen.
- **List size sweet spot: 50–4,000.** Below 50 too small; above 4,000 narrow it.
- **First homework: 100 ideal prospects, paste only the LinkedIn URL** into the sheet as an "anchor." Fill the rest of the data later or outsource it.

**WHY:** smaller companies = easier to reach the right person + easier to break in. Start small, go up.

**2026 flag:** Sales Nav still valid, but for us **Gooseworks/scraping tooling replaces the manual scan**. Same filter logic, faster. The "describe-themselves" industry trick and the 80%-fit / 50–4,000 sizing rules are timeless.

## 4. Outreach copy — the single goal: de-risk the first conversation (DE)

**The One Simple Task.** Outreach does NOT close deals or build know/like/trust. It does *one* thing: de-risk the first conversation. The prospect must be able to tick most of four boxes (not all):
1. **"You're in my world."** (most important — kills the "cold" feeling fast)
2. **"You understand me."** (why specialization + right terms of art matter)
3. **"This seems interesting."** (enough to warrant a chat, no more)
4. **"I won't feel uncomfortable."** (no forced sales process, no time-waste)

The hallmark of doing this right: replies that say **"I usually don't respond to cold emails, but this seemed different."** All drip/multichannel/software is just a means to check these boxes. Timeless; this is the north star.

**Six guidelines for the copy itself:**
1. **Focus on the prospect.** Most cold email is 80% sender / 20% prospect. Flip it. Body = about them; talk about your agency *below the signature*. Name-drop authority tastefully, self-effacingly, never lead with brags.
2. **Be digestible.** People judge an email by its *shape* before reading a word. The body doesn't carry the whole story — subject line, PS, signature, LinkedIn profile, and follow-ups share the load. Caveat: visible research buys permission to write longer.
3. **Be specific.** Kill adjectives and superlatives ("award-winning," "best of the industry" = dead on arrival). Name real clients and the exact thing you do. Specificity lets bad-fits opt themselves out.
4. **Pattern interrupt.** Break the auto-delete reflex. Be casual, unorthodox, irreverent — profanity if the audience fits. "The most successful campaigns read like emails from a friend." Let people qualify out via the copy.
5. **MVF — Minimum Viable Friction.** Too little friction ("Do you need agency services?") → polite no. Too much ("write me a paragraph") → ignored. Sweet spot for a new campaign: ask for a **short brainstorm**, or a single answerable question ("How many video projects do you run a year?"). Email reply can be a down-sell when the call ask is declined.
6. **Read aloud.** Every time. Clarity, conversational tone, voice match.

**Context levers (Market / Message / Sender / Medium):**
- **Sender** = a **high-authority specialist** (the person who'll be on the call) or someone selling time with them. The BDR model is weak in marketing/advertising because you're selling "a brainstorm + ideas," not a demo. Keep the sender's **LinkedIn tagline focused on the prospect's vertical** (e.g. "CPG strategist") — it does silent work checking box #1.
- **Medium mix (their numbers):** ~**80% of booked meetings come from email**, ~**20% from LinkedIn**, **5–10%** involve phone (warm only, never cold). Email is the workhorse: stable, schedulable, creative room.

**Snippet / personalization system:**
- Spreadsheet "snippets" column = one short researched line dropped straight into the email. **Start with ONE piece of info per prospect** ("less is more"); scale later.
- Snippet sources: Google News on the company, press releases, LinkedIn posts/articles, new hires, funding rounds, teammate mentions, **industry events** (adds a human angle).
- Use a **company nickname** (drop "LLC" etc.) so it reads like an insider, not scraped junk.
- Subject line = **prospect-first** (their name/reference before yours).

**Verbatim templates (riff, don't copy):**
- *Long/personalized (won Google, HP, Microsoft):* references a recent article they wrote, compliments their expertise, asks how they're shifting strategy, names recent wins, asks for a 15-min chat + Calendly, ends "if this isn't a good fit, let me know."
- *Short/non-personalized:* "I've had close to 100 conversations with professionals in our space… here's the distilled version. [link] If you're on the fence with any of these, hit reply." (the "I'm busy, you're busy" tone) — **DE's point: opposite copy styles both worked. There is no one right email.**
- *Pattern-interrupt (won ~a dozen agencies):* "...we did compelling work with the likes of Walmart and Optima batteries… Do you guys have a dedicated business development person? If not, sorry to bug you. If so, I'm curious how you're supplying them with opportunity."

All timeless. For us, the snippet engine is where AI does the heavy lifting at scale — but keep it to one sharp, *true* detail per prospect.

## 5. The outbound system / cadence (DE)

**Mental model — "galaxies":** every no-relationship prospect lives in the **Outbound Galaxy**. The instant they reply (even "no"), they exit to the **Sales Galaxy** (your CRM). Platforms auto-pull repliers from sequences.

**The cadence (LinkedIn-first, email-driven):**
1. **LinkedIn connection request** — light or no CTA ("hope we can chat in the future"). **Do NOT send a LinkedIn follow-up message** (too common, doesn't work). Manual connecting is safer than automation (ToS risk).
2. **Wait 2–7 days.**
3. **Email drip** (same thread throughout):
   - **Email 1** — light, ideally no links (deliverability). Reference the LinkedIn touch even if they didn't accept: *"I think we connected on LinkedIn, we're in your space."* This makes them check your (vertical-focused) profile = warmth. **~80% of meetings come from email 1.**
   - **Email 2** — slightly longer, de-risks the offer, can carry a link (short video / case study / blog). "Did you get this? Here's me shedding more light on what we do."
   - **Email 3 (down-sell / Hail Mary)** — "Couldn't break through before, so let me try something different — a couple quick questions to make sure I'm being heard." Goal: a reply + market intel.

**Stack (2019–2020 era — names dated, logic current):**
- Research: **LinkedIn Sales Navigator**.
- Enrichment: **Lead IQ** (validates emails first, so you don't research dead data). Or guess conventions (first.last@, finitiallast@).
- Sending: a **dedicated outbound platform** — Reply.io or Woodpecker. NOT Mailchimp/HubSpot newsletters ("cutting a steak with a butter knife"). Not Outreach (too pricey).
- CRM: anything (Pipedrive, HubSpot, Close, even a sheet). Proposals: Proposify / Better Proposals.
- **Automation target ~50%.** Front-load the work into data hygiene + research; once running, repeat. 100% = "set and forget" (avoid for high-value work); 0% = reinventing the wheel.

**Deliverability infra (the most-aged section):**
- **Vanity domain** (e.g. agency.co, redirects to main) to protect main-domain reputation. DE says nobody ever flags it as fake.
- **DKIM, SPF, DMARC** set up.
- **Warm cold domains** (MailWarm or similar) before sending.
- Ensure all replies route to ONE monitored inbox (lost-lead risk).

**2026 flags:** vanity domain + SPF/DKIM/DMARC + warmup are now **table stakes and stricter** (Google/Yahoo enforce DMARC; one-domain-many-inbox patterns get flagged). Volumes per inbox are lower than 2020. Treat the *logic* (protect reputation, low volume, high relevance) as current; treat the *specific tools/volumes* as needing a 2026 refresh. The "ship imperfect to get momentum" stance is timeless.

## 6. Reading replies → moving to booked call (DE)

**Reply diagnosis (every reply is a signal):**
- **"Not interested" / "no thanks"** = the MOST common reply *even in winning campaigns*. Good news: you reached the right person. Problem is copy/offer, not targeting. **High reply rate + low booking = fix copy, not data.**
- **Right person, wrong company** (they moved on) = data-sourcing issue.
- **High bounce rate** = data hygiene issue, narrow the list.
- **Deferral ("not now, later")** = second-best outcome. Move to CRM, follow up in **6–12 months**, and ask: *"Do you mind me asking what will be different at that time?"* (so you return informed).
- **"Shed more light" / send portfolio** = gut-check it's genuine, send it, then **re-ask for the call**: "Happy to answer anything else — otherwise we'd love to learn about your situation. Here's my calendar."
- **"Interested"** = the goal. **Move to CRM immediately** (don't lose them — "special eggs"). Send a calendar link. **No reply in 24h → call within a few days**, remind them how you connected. Persistence is accepted once interest is shown.

**Benchmarks (laser-pointer, contextual not absolute):**
- **Open rate ~30%** target (driven by deliverability first, then subject + preview line). Below → check spam/data. Affected by list size, company size, industry.
- **Reply rate ~10%** target. Pattern rule: feedback once = note it; **twice+ = investigate and consider changing.**
- **Meeting/booking rate ~1–3%** of contacts short-term (more book later via re-engagement).
- Activity math: **~10–50 pieces of custom outreach → 1 meeting; ~10–20 meetings → 1 closed deal.**

**Falsifiable KPI:** stop tracking vague "leads" (MQL/SQL = "BS"). Track **"book deployments"** — did a time hit the calendar, yes/no. Easy to verify, can't be gamed.

**Case-study proof point:** SF brand agency, first-ever outbound, selling 6–7 figure branding to Fortune 500. Year one: **~40 meetings + ~30 warm deferrals in CRM**, ~one meeting every 1–2 weeks, **closed a $125K initial engagement** (real-estate conglomerate). Clusters of ~80–700 prospects (~200 typical), ~20 clusters.

All timeless. These benchmarks are the realistic shape of a high-ticket outbound funnel; use them to set 90-day expectations and avoid panic-pivoting.

## 7. Hit campaigns + re-engagement (the long-term compounding) (DE)

- **Hit campaigns:** most campaigns book a meeting every 1–2 weeks; occasionally one "goes off like gangbusters" (5/10/20 meetings). Mistake = assuming a hit lasts forever and recycling the copy. Keep experimenting to open the door to more hits.
- **The "digital networking" hit (highest-ROI play for us):** target *employees of past clients* — "we did marketing at [former client], I think our paths crossed, what are you up to now at [company], let's connect." People are excited to reconnect even if they barely remember. **This is the warm-outbound goldmine.**
- **Re-engagement (DE: "the area that produces the greatest opportunities"):** non-repliers aren't dead. Re-engage **30–90 days** out. **Prioritize opens/clicks/forwarders** (high-touch them: phone, direct mail). Fuel it with **thought leadership / new work / events**, NOT the same offer. Tactics: **switch the sender** (different person, even different gender works "for social reasons"); bring in firepower ("our CPG specialist wanted a brainstorm"). Over-90-days = often treat as a fresh prospect but change the angle.
- **CRM discipline:** keep a "closed-no" section with tiers (golden/silver/bronze eggs) and a **filter for everyone not touched in 30/90 days** with the reason to circle back ("new CMO arriving," "new website launching").

**WHY for us:** Chaga is ex-DTC operator. His **past client/colleague network = the fastest first-cash channel** (warm digital-networking campaign), gated behind filling the credibility-anchor blocker (real past DTC store details).

## 8. Relationship-driven outbound at scale (DE bonus)

When direct outreach to CMO/VP Marketing gets no reply, layer in **commonalities** as the way in:

- **Indirect connectors** — high-level people at the target who aren't the marketing buyer but share a tie (alma mater, ex-employer, same city). Largest group by volume; calls "almost always worth having" for: (1) volume ("set your watch to getting meetings"), (2) market intel (one of only two ways, besides RFPs, to learn if a real need exists), (3) authority (a warm referral can't be faked).
- **Hopscotch approach:** good call with the connector → research the real decision maker (Sales Nav "decision makers" filter) → warm email referencing the connector. *"Had a great call with Samia, your head of HR — wanted to connect… we're in the same city… would love a quick chat to learn what you're up to and make a new connection."* Implies you're not pitching. Systematized (a few/day), not mass-scaled.
- **Historic-client commonality:** Sales Nav → "past company" = a client you're happy to name → narrow by your geography → narrow by current title "marketing." (Example: PepsiCo 160k past employees → +Chicago = 2,000 → +marketing = 123, a one-week campaign.) Copy: "Our paths might've crossed — we did great work for Pepsi, saw you're right here in Chicago, curious what you're up to at [company], open to a call?"
- **Lego blocks:** past-employer + alumni (filter by school) + professional groups (CMO/marketing/social) are interchangeable commonalities to stack for personalization. (Alumni works best with a big school.)

Timeless and high-fit. For an AI agency, this is a near-zero-cost warm channel.

## 9. Thought leadership as the fuel (DE bonus)

- **Highest leverage = plug into *existing* audiences** (podcasts, publications, guest posts), not building your own from scratch (no blank-page problem, audience already built). Podcasts framed as durable.
- **Three campaign buckets:** (1) Industry-focus (where your target clients listen), (2) Marketing/Advertising (peers, idea-hungry, industry-agnostic), (3) Out-of-the-box (adjacent worlds with a tie-in).
- **Angles that land:** controversial opinion, insider secret (where brands waste money), war story (anonymized), personal transformation, prophecy (where the space is heading). Lean into **storytelling**.
- **Outreach:** reference an *actual* episode (do the homework), pitch a specific edgy conversation, mix in irreverence. **10–20 targets/week.** Tools: Listen Notes (podcasts), Ninja Outreach (podcasts+blogs).
- **Post-placement = don't be lazy:** re-engage cold non-repliers (filter: contacted 2wks ago, no reply, opened 3x) with "we just got interviewed on [show], wanted to run it by you" — you now arrive with far more authority. Linking to an interview in outreach beats a case study. This re-engagement message is highly automatable.

Timeless. For Adwield this doubles as the credibility/authority builder that the cold copy leans on.

## 10. Selling in uncertain / tight times (DE webinar — relevant to cost-sensitive DTC in 2026)

- **Survival actions:** talk to clients 1:1 by phone (reveals more than email); offer *tangible* help not just discounts; re-engage 30/90/180-day-old prospects by asking which way they went ("ask for advice, get money; ask for money, get advice"); deal in facts not rumors (most agencies wrongly assume their market is dead off two data points — prospect voraciously to know); **band together** with non-competing agencies/software/consultants.
- **Repackage, don't price-cut:** lower the *entry point* with a project, not a retainer (buyers fear open-ended commitment). E.g. a **$25K audit → $3K one-day workshop** that leads into bigger work. Ask the prospect: **"How would you like to buy? What would make getting started simpler?"** Sell into a **labor gap** (work that *must* get done) over a knowledge gap; expertise becomes the icing. Bring a specialist onto call 1–2 ("firepower").
- **Healthy pipeline mindset (3 groups):** the largest = don't know you; the middle = know/like/trust you; the smallest = ready to buy. You must **constantly grow the middle group** — referral-addicted agencies skip it and starve when budgets reopen.
- **90–180-day principle (Jeb Blount):** what you do (or skip) today shows up in results 90–180 days out. Prospecting has a half-life; stopping = backsliding downhill. Matters more in a downturn.
- **Crisis copy (Nate Wright):** lead with empathy, never bury it; **pause all automations** and audit tone (a "this is my last follow-up" breakup email reads as manipulative); **avoid sweeping assumptions** ("I'm sure you're worried about…" — experiences vary wildly); slow down and write fresh, don't update old copy; give first with no CTA.

Highly relevant: DTC operators in 2026 are margin-squeezed. The **project-not-retainer entry point** and **"how would you like to buy"** map straight onto our offer ladder and the budget-conscious ICP.

## 11. Maria Wendt — transferable scraps only (MW)

The course is an **inbound Instagram comment-to-DM / ManyChat funnel for selling courses and products**. It is **not an agency B2B outbound or sales-call system** and does not cover discovery calls, objection handling on calls, or reply-to-close for high-ticket services. Treat it as out of scope for the core mandate. What *does* transfer:

- **Comment-to-DM trigger as a low-friction inbound capture:** post with a CTA ("comment [keyword] below"), bot auto-replies publicly + DMs the link. Engagement on the CTA post spikes reach via the algorithm (a "pitch post" with even 10 comments = ~5x normal engagement → more reach → more comments). Relevant if Adwield runs an *organic content → DM → call* top-of-funnel. **Mechanic, not strategy.**
- **New-vs-returning segmentation (condition + tag):** first-time responder gets an intro link; returning ("warmer") responder gets a different, further-down-funnel message. Same logic as DE's outbound-vs-reengagement split, applied to DMs. Useful pattern for any automated reply flow.
- **Objection-buster content format:** a short reel answering ONE *very specific* objection ("you can't make money on IG without a big following — false, here's how"), then a direct CTA. Specificity beats general (general is "oversaturated"). Maps to our hook/ad content and to addressing the ICP's #1 objection up front.
- **Social-proof-first principle:** "to sell something you must prove you have it." Show the result/transformation, not claims. (She uses ChatGPT to brainstorm niche-specific proof types.) Reinforces DE's "be specific, name real wins."
- **Cart/checkout-recovery branch:** tag anyone who hit checkout but didn't buy → wait → offer a coupon, with an edge-case branch for "I already bought" (offer bonuses or a refund path). A reusable *recovery-automation* pattern, transferable to any abandoned high-intent action.
- **Urgency CTA + "if it's not easy I'm not doing it":** limited-spots framing; bias to simple, repeatable plays. Mindset echo of DE's "ship imperfect."

**Flag:** everything platform-specific here (ManyChat UI, IG algorithm tactics, $15 fees, 55-minute coupon delays, reels calendar Tue/Wed/Thu) is tactical and channel-bound. Don't import as a sales methodology. The DE material is the methodology; MW contributes a couple of automation patterns for an organic-social front door if we ever run one.

## Net for Adwield (synthesis-ready)

1. **Methodology spine = DE.** Laser-pointer outbound, one researched snippet per prospect, four-box "de-risk the first conversation" copy, LinkedIn-tagline-as-proof, email-thread drip, reply-diagnosis → calendar.
2. **Fastest first cash = warm digital-networking** to Chaga's past DTC clients/colleagues (gated on the credibility-anchor blocker). Then historic-client + indirect-connector commonality plays.
3. **Offer the entry point as a project, not a retainer** ("how would you like to buy"), tuned to margin-squeezed DTC.
4. **Expectations:** ~30% open, ~10% reply, ~1–3% book; ~10–50 customizations/meeting, ~10–20 meetings/close. Don't pivot on one data point; optimize copy (not data) when replies are high but bookings low.
5. **AI is the edge on the snippet engine + scraping** (Gooseworks), compressing DE's manual research — speed, not cost, is the moat.
6. **Deliverability needs a 2026 refresh** (DMARC enforcement, lower per-inbox volume); DE's specific tools/numbers are dated, the logic isn't.
7. **MW = optional organic-social front door mechanics only.** Not a sales framework.
