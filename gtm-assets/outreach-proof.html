Outreach & Sales — Proof File

Every tactic the Adwield/Tideover outreach system relies on, traced to the operators and datasets that corroborate it. PROVEN where 5+ independent successful sources agree, WEAKER where 1-2 do, and flagged where our current playbook or scripts diverge from the proven consensus.

What this is. The reference Chaga reads to trust that the outreach and sales language is real, not invented. Built from the seven research briefs in _outreach-proof-research/, the outreach-playbook.md, and the copy-standard.md exemplars. Every claim below names the operator, their proof of scale, the specific thing they assert, and the data or link. Verdicts are by count of independent corroborating sources, not by how good the line sounds.

How to read the verdict labels.

  • PROVEN — 5+ independent successful operators or large-N datasets agree. Build on it without hedging.
  • STRONG (3-4) — corroborated but short of the 5-source bar. Trust it; note it isn't yet bulletproof.
  • WEAKER (1-2) — one or two sources, often self-reported or single-anecdote. Use directionally, never quote the number as fact.
  • DIVERGENCE — our playbook or scripts currently disagree with the proven consensus. The revise pass should change one or the other.

Source hygiene carried from the briefs. "Operator first, teacher second" is the trust spine. A course-seller's tactic can be sound while their income number is marketing. Self-reported revenue, ClickFunnels "Two Comma" plaques, and platform marketing percentages are flagged at the point of use, never treated as audited. Large-N send datasets (Saleshandy 53.1M, Belkins 16.5M, Woodpecker 20M, Hunter 11M, Instantly "billions") are the ground truth the operator claims get checked against.

TACTIC 1 — Cold-email structure (short body, opener → problem → proof → CTA, one ask, no link)

What our system does. Playbook §4.1 and copy-standard #3: plain-text, under 80 words, opener that says the quiet part → the artifact (proof) → one soft interest-based CTA, no calendar link in email 1.

Corroborating sources:

  1. 30MPC (Farrokh/Cero) — most-followed no-nonsense B2B sales brand, tactics tested on datasets. Claim, verbatim: cold email is "under 100 words, digestible on mobile WITHOUT scrolling" = ~3-4 sentences, structure Opener → Problem → Proof → CTA ("the Reply Method"). The numbers behind it: pitching reduces replies up to 57%; leading with the prospect's problem +20%; social proof +41%; a value offer vs asking for time +28%. (30MPC formula)
  2. Saleshandy — 53.1M emails. Body under 80 words (50-80 optimal); one soft CTA generated 78% more positive replies than a hard CTA; multiple CTAs = worst. (saleshandy)
  3. Belkins — 16.5M emails. Concise value prop is part of the 5-10% reply recipe; long mail (600+ words) sits at the bottom (4% reply). (belkins)
  4. Built For B2B — 10,000+ campaigns, 50 industries. By length: 50-75 words = 12% reply, 75-125 = 10%, 125-200 = 7%, 200+ = 2%. Hard data behind "keep it short." (builtforb2b)
  5. Alex Berman / Experiment 27 — the 5-part skeleton (subject → compliment → case study → single CTA → signature) and the 3C ("why me / why believe you / what do you want"). Built a $400K-ARR-in-30-days agency on it. (Hunter teardown, BlitzMetrics)
  6. Forum consensus (Cameron Scully thread, r/IndieHackers) — top unprompted skeptical reply to a long me-first email: "your email body is too long. your intro — talk about them not you." The market polices length and self-focus without being asked. (IndieHackers)
  7. Instantly — 2026 benchmark names "under 80 words" as an elite-sender trait; one clear ask per email. (instantly)

Verdict: PROVEN. Seven independent sources (two operators, four large-N datasets, one forum consensus) converge on: short body, problem-led, single soft CTA. The 78%-lift soft-CTA number (Saleshandy 53.1M) is the strongest single stat. Our copy-standard #3 already nails this.

Divergence — minor word-count target. 30MPC and Belkins/Built-For-B2B's 50-75 word band suggest our "<80 words" ceiling could tighten to a 50-75 target for the cold-email exemplars; copy-standard #3 runs ~70 words, inside the band, so this is a tune not a fix.

TACTIC 2 — The opener / hook (one true specific observation, says the quiet part, curiosity over pitch)

What our system does. Playbook §3.2 (S.I.P.E.), §3.5 ({{hook}} bar: true + specific + says the quiet part), Principle 6 (curiosity is the on-switch). Copy-standard #1 opener: "your whole account is riding on the one [hero-product] testimonial ad."

Corroborating sources:

  1. 30MPC — pitching reduces replies up to 57%; leading with the prospect's problem +20%. The opener's job is to name their problem, not your offer. (30MPC)
  2. Sam McKenna / SMYKM — element 1-3: subject "so especially unique to them it only makes sense to one person," first sentence kills "I hope this finds you well," reported 43% open / 20% reply vs 6% / 0.9% industry. 250+ enterprise clients behind the method. Number is her own claim (flag); the structure is corroborated by her operator record. (Apollo)
  3. Hunter — buyer survey, 217 decision-makers: 71% name lack of relevance as the #1 reason they ignore cold email; impersonality 43%. The opener has to prove you looked at them. (hunter)
  4. Eric Nowoslawski — the "Creative Ideas" play opens with an AI-written custom first line drawn from a predetermined set, purpose-built to clear spam filters and signal real relevance. 1-in-70 positive replies on the reputation-management case. (his Substack)
  5. Daniel Fazio — his most-quoted rule: a researched compliment ("personalization/compliments increase replies ~3x"). Opener = short, researched, about them. Sent 100k+ himself. 3x is a rule of thumb, not measured. (his Medium)
  6. Jasmin Alić — #1 LinkedIn creator; "specificity over vague" verbatim ("Write better emails" → "92% open rate emails"); draft the hook first, before the post. Content-craft, ports to the opener. (hey-jay resources)
  7. Justin Welsh — cold DM touch 1 leads with a specific observation and a concrete improvement, asks for nothing; attributes a $50K contract to the sequence. (Welsh cold DM)

Verdict: PROVEN. Seven sources agree the opener is a true, specific, them-first observation, and that curiosity/relevance beats a pitch. The Hunter 71%-relevance stat and the 30MPC -57%-for-pitching stat are the load-bearing data.

Divergence — "dead openers" list is corroborated, keep enforcing it. Our §2 dead-opener ban ("Loved your recent post," "Congrats on the promotion," "I hope this finds you well") is directly confirmed by McKenna (kill "hope this finds you well") and Hunter (impersonal = ignored). No change; this is a strength worth protecting against future drift.

TACTIC 3 — Signal / trigger targeting and personalization (one fresh signal, WHO before WHAT, segment honestly)

What our system does. Playbook §2 ("signal-first beats sequence-first," one fresh signal per prospect), §3.6 hook-sourcing engine, Principle 8 (specificity = proof of effort). This is the single biggest lever the system bets on.

Corroborating sources:

  1. Woodpecker — 20M emails. Advanced personalization 17% reply vs 7% without — a clean 10-point A/B lift on a real dataset. The most trustworthy personalization stat in the set. (woodpecker)
  2. Belkins — 16.5M emails. Trigger/signal personalization → 15-25% reply; C-level responds 6.4% when outreach shows real account knowledge vs ~3-5% generic. (belkins)
  3. Saleshandy — 53.1M emails. Only ~5% of senders personalize each email; those see 2-3x the replies. (saleshandy)
  4. Hunter — 217-buyer survey. 71% ignore cold email for lack of relevance — relevance is a targeting problem, not a mail-merge problem. (hunter)
  5. The 464,000-email SaaS engine [Reddit r/SaaS via aggregator] — narrowing ICP from "B2B SaaS" to "sales-assisted companies, $500K-3M ARR, already using cold email" lifted reply from 0.2% to 2.3-3.3% — a 10x swing from targeting alone, copy unchanged. The clearest single evidence that WHO beats WHAT. Username stripped by aggregator (flag). (prospeo)
  6. Eric Nowoslawski — merges 20+ databases in Clay; targeted businesses at 3.5-4.5 star ratings (the band with a felt problem + budget) for 1-in-70 positive replies. (Smartlead case study)
  7. Nick Abraham — 3 list plays: scrape competitor LinkedIn engagers, scrape competitor followers, target companies hiring SDR/BDR roles (buying-intent signal). (Startup Spells)
  8. Apollo (re-citing Belkins/Sopro) — trigger-based (job change, funding, tech adoption) beats persona templates; advanced personalization 18% vs 9% generic. Apollo re-cites third parties (flag). (apollo)

Verdict: PROVEN, and the strongest-corroborated tactic in the file. Eight sources, four of them large-N datasets, converge: a real fresh trigger doubles-to-10x's reply rate, and fake/token personalization is now a liability. Our §3.6 hook-sourcing engine (Meta Ad Library, review themes, Kickstarter end-dates) is the defensible version of this.

Divergence — none on the principle; one execution risk. The briefs warn that AI-generated "personalization" is now worse than none (Instantly, Hunter). Playbook §3.6 already mandates "a tool pulls the raw signal; a human writes the hook." Keep that human-writes-the-hook rule absolute. The only thing that would break this tactic is letting AI write the variable line.

TACTIC 4 — Follow-up cadence (3-5 touches over ~20 days, follow-ups carry ~half of replies, stop at 4-6)

What our system does. Playbook §4.1 cadence: 3-5 emails, Day 1 / 4 / 8 / 13 / 18-20, each adds new info, breakup is useful not guilt-trip.

Corroborating sources:

  1. Saleshandy — 53.1M emails. Follow-ups generate 44% of positive replies (FU1 = 26.4%, FU2 = 10.9%, FU3+ = 7.2%). Optimal 4-6 emails over 20-21 days. (saleshandy)
  2. Belkins — 16.5M emails. First follow-up lifts replies up to 49%; but email 4 = response drops 55% and spam complaints climb to 1.6% (above Google's 0.3% ceiling = domain-killer). The bearish counterweight: follow-ups decay faster than Saleshandy shows. (belkins)
  3. Woodpecker — 20M emails. 1-3 emails = 9% reply, 4-7 emails = 27% reply (campaign-level, magnitude generous — flag). Direction: more touches up to ~6 wins. (woodpecker)
  4. Hunter — 11M emails. Campaigns with follow-ups 4.9% vs without 3.0%. (hunter)
  5. Backlinko/Pitchbox — 12M emails (2019). One follow-up +65.8% replies; 3+ messages highest overall. (backlinko)
  6. Instantly — 2026. First-touch 58% of replies / follow-ups 42%; 1-3 follow-ups over 7-14 days. (instantly)
  7. Smartlead — recommends FU1 +2-3 days, FU2 +4-5, FU3 +7, FU4 +10-14. (smartlead frequency)

Verdict: PROVEN. Seven sources, six large-N. Follow-ups carry ~42-44% of replies; the first follow-up is the single most valuable message after the opener; stop at 4-6 because email 4+ buys spam complaints, not replies.

Divergence — flag against any "12-touch" guru advice. Hormozi's spoken "8-12 touches" and some course-sellers' long sequences are contradicted by every dataset here. Our playbook's 3-5 is correct and on the safe side. Hold it. Do not let a future revise pad the sequence past 6.

TACTIC 5 — The soft, single, interest-based CTA (ask for interest not time, defer the calendar link)

What our system does. Playbook §2 ("ask for interest, not time"), §4.1 step 5, copy-standard warm-reply tree (defer pricing and calendar to the call). Copy-standard #3 closes "If the output holds up, that's the whole pitch" — interest, not a meeting demand.

Corroborating sources:

  1. Saleshandy — 53.1M emails. One soft CTA = 78% more positive replies than a hard CTA; multiple CTAs = worst. The cornerstone stat. (saleshandy)
  2. 30MPC — a value offer (vs asking for their time) +28% replies. CTA rule, verbatim: "don't ask for their time. Offer value." (30MPC)
  3. Sam McKenna — element 7: always a CTA, but never propose a specific time or drop a calendar link in the first message. "Do you have time over the next week or two? I'll send an invite." Buyer convenience first. (Apollo)
  4. Daniel Fazio — "asking for a reply (and not including a link) increases replies ~3x." No link = a deliverability and response lever both. (his Medium)
  5. The 150-brand video campaign [Reddit via aggregator] — first email is a pure permission-ask ("Want me to send it?"), no link, no pitch; 34% reply, then 60% of video recipients booked. (prospeo)
  6. Gong — call-recording analysis: interest-based CTA ~30% success. (gong)
  7. Lemlist — rewrite the CTA into micro-commitments (yes/no, A/B, permission-based "should I send X?"). Method sound; Lemlist numbers discounted. (lemlist openers)

Verdict: PROVEN. Seven sources. Soft single interest-based CTA, defer the calendar link, no raw link in touch 1. The 78% (Saleshandy) and +28% (30MPC) numbers are the spine.

Divergence — copy-standard exemplars carry a link in touch 1. Adwield exemplars #1-3 include [link] to the artifact in the first message. Fazio's "no link = ~3x" and the permission-ask pattern (150-brand campaign, McKenna) say the higher-converting move is to ask permission first ("I built you a 15-sec ad of [product] — want me to send it?") and deliver the link on the "yes." This is a genuine tension: our artifact is the value and showing it immediately is the whole proof-first thesis, but the permission-gated version tests higher across the briefs. Revise pass should A/B the permission-ask variant of #1-3 rather than assume the embedded link is optimal. The warm-reply tree already defers pricing and calendar correctly.

TACTIC 6 — The DM approach (DM is traffic not a pitch, profile closes, give-first, depth over volume)

What our system does. Playbook §4.2/§4.3 (dial the profile first, connection request no note, DM on a real trigger, give-first), copy-standard #1-2 and #5-6 (artifact-first DMs).

Corroborating sources:

  1. Justin Welsh — $4.15M/yr solo, zero employees, verifiable follower/testimonial counts. 3-Touch-With-Value cold DM: touch 1 gives, asks nothing; never open with "jump on a call" or "pick your brain." $50K contract attributed. (Welsh cold DM)
  2. Sam McKenna — 250+ enterprise clients. "Quality over quantity. Spend 80% of your time on 20% of your prospects." Start at ~20 messages/week, not hundreds. Defer the calendar link. (Scratchpad)
  3. Daniel Fazio — core principle corroborated everywhere: "the cold DM or cold email is only a traffic mechanism — the magic happens when prospects click your profile, see your offer, and act from there." (profile)
  4. Clifton Sellers (Hampton-vetted) — ~100 DMs/day, profile + content do the work; ladder to $30-50K/mo then seven-figure run rate. The high-volume-thin-personalization model. (Hampton)
  5. 2026 LinkedIn algorithm data — connection requests without a note get 55-68% acceptance vs 28-45% with a generic note; automated invites dying (60%→30%); "sends" (1:1 DM shares) are now the strongest ranking signal. The DM channel is more important in 2026, not less. (hyperclapper, reactin)
  6. X DM engagement-then-DM cadence [automationlinks + Tremblay] — keep 15-20 live prospects, warm ~1 week (like + thoughtful comment), first DM 100% about them. Cadence corroborated across 3 sources; the response-multiplier numbers are single-source, discount. (automationlinks)
  7. Matt Gray — "specific compliment → open question → permission ask → channel move." Framework usable, all his revenue/impression numbers single-weak-source, not citable. (founderos)

Verdict: PROVEN (structure) / STRONG (depth-vs-volume). The "DM is traffic, profile closes, give first" structure is 7-source PROVEN. The depth-over-volume choice for our ticket size is matched to deal size (McKenna/Welsh depth vs Sellers/Fazio volume), so it's a correct judgment (STRONG) rather than a single proven rule.

Divergence — profile-as-funnel is under-built relative to its proof. Every credible DM source (Welsh, McKenna, Fazio, Sellers) says the DM only buys a profile click; the profile does the closing. Playbook §4.2 step 1 names this but the system has no proof that the Adwield/Tideover profiles actually carry one offer + visible proof before outbound goes live. Revise pass: make "profile/featured section converts" a hard pre-launch gate, not a step. Also confirmed: §3.4's "connection request, no note" is right per the 2026 data, except a hyper-specific <180-char note is the one exception (currently the playbook says no note flatly — tighten to "no note, or one ultra-specific <180-char note").

TACTIC 7 — The discovery framework (frame → gap → consequence → outcome, prospect closes themselves)

What our system does. Referenced by v1-sales-call-kit (the call map: opener/frame → pain-dig → Track confirmation → pitch → guarantee → ask). The briefs propose explicit upgrades.

Corroborating sources:

  1. Alex Hormozi — C.L.O.S.E.R — the script his portfolio sales teams ran. Clarify why they came → Label the problem → Overview past pain → Sell the vacation not the plane → Explain concerns → Reinforce. "Sell the vacation, not the plane flight." Hard-verified scale ($46.2M Gym Launch exit). ([brief 03, AH section])
  2. Jeremy Miner — NEPQ — DSA-ranked 45th-highest-earning salesperson worldwide. 7 question types; the missing piece for us is consequence questions ("What happens if you don't do anything and your problem keeps getting worse over the next 6 months?") — make them voice the cost, don't supply it. ([brief 03, JM section])
  3. Cole Gordon — Belief Ladder — personally closed $10M+ before 25. 7 beliefs (pain, doubt, cost, desire, money, support, trust) all must be true; run it as a silent pre-close checklist. ([brief 03, CG section])
  4. Josh Nelson — agency close — highest-fit (built/sold the exact agency we sell). Set the agenda AND ask for a decision by end of call to pre-empt "I need to think about it." Gap analysis pivot: "Can I show you where your current strategy is falling short?" States a 41% close rate (single-source, self-reported, no sample size — ceiling not benchmark, flag). ([brief 03, JN section])
  5. Sam Ovens — real operator past ($7M/yr consultancy before the course). The transferable lesson: the discovery script is a tested, iterated instrument, not improvised; log recurring objections, version the script. (Growth Models)
  6. 2026 buyer-skepticism data — only 26% of B2B reps hit quota in 2025 (vs historical ~70%); the pressure-close era is dead, discovery-heavy self-close survives. Tailwind for our proof-first, no-hard-close motion. ([brief 03 §1])

Verdict: PROVEN (the spine) / STRONG (the exact merged sequence). The five-move spine (frame → discover the gap → make them voice the consequence → present as the bridge → price after desire) is the through-line across four operators who all did it at size, plus the 2026 quota data — PROVEN. The specific 13-step merged call map in brief 03 §7 is a synthesis (STRONG, not yet run live).

Divergence — two named gaps in the current call kit. Brief 03 flags (a) no consequence question — the kit surfaces buying temperature but never makes the prospect voice the cost of the dying winner; add Miner's consequence question. And (b) no decision pre-frame in the agenda — Nelson's "by the end we'll both know, and I'll ask you to decide" is the cheapest fix for ghosting. Revise pass: add both to v1-sales-call-kit.

TACTIC 8 — The close (price after desire, anchor to their P&L, assumptive next-action, never discount)

What our system does. Copy-standard warm-reply tree ("a number now anchors against nothing," defer to the call); locked rules "never discount the retainer to close," anchor "$2.5K out of a single profitable day of ad spend."

Corroborating sources:

  1. Alex Hormozi — price reveal late, after desire ("S" in CLOSER); by the time you state a number the prospect has verbalized the outcome's worth. ([brief 03, AH])
  2. Cole Gordon — "commit phase": get a yes on the logic before you ever quote. "Money is very easy to handle if they 100% believe this is what they need." A price objection is an unbuilt belief upstream. ([brief 03, CG])
  3. Jeremy Miner — price objection handled by Clarify first ("How do you mean, too expensive?"), never defended head-on. ([brief 03, JM])
  4. Josh Nelson — present offer with pricing/deliverables/examples together, then assumptive close: "Let's schedule your kickoff. Does Thursday at 3 work?" Action-based, not "do you want to buy." ([brief 03, JN])
  5. Andrew Pierno — "100 emails → 10 meetings → close 6-7 ... numbers holding strong even as we've doubled our price." Climbed free → $500 → $1K → $1.5K → $2K. Real close-rate-at-scale proof, and price went up without breaking the funnel. (Failory)
  6. Pricing-after-desire consensus — all four sales operators (AH, JM, CG, JN) independently converge: never quote into a vacuum, anchor against the outcome's value not a competitor rate card, a price objection is a belief gap not a money problem. The highest-confidence finding in brief 03 (corroborated 4 ways). ([brief 03 §6])

Verdict: PROVEN. Six sources, four sales operators in full agreement plus an operator who proved price can rise without breaking close rate. Our locked rules (price late, anchor to their P&L, never discount) are exactly the consensus.

Divergence — none. This is the best-aligned tactic in the system. The only watch-item: brief 03's 2026 note that the "send a deck / let me get pricing over" move is weaker than ever — price lives on the call. Our warm-reply tree already defers pricing to the call rather than emailing it, which is correct. Protect that.

TACTIC 9 — Objection handling (clarify the real objection before answering, isolate, diffuse)

What our system does. Copy-standard reply-handlers (#4 "AI ads are slop," #7 "we already have a helpdesk") and the warm-reply objection tree; playbook §5 locked objection counters.

Corroborating sources:

  1. Jeremy Miner — Clarify → Discuss → Diffuse — the most-cited, best-corroborated piece of NEPQ. Never answer an objection; question it first ("When you say X, what do you mean by that?"). 4+ sources incl. 7thLevel's own site. ([brief 03, JM])
  2. Josh Nelson — Acknowledge → Isolate → Handle. "I need to think about it" → "What would you be thinking about most: price, results, or value?" Isolate the real objection before answering. ([brief 03, JN])
  3. Cole Gordon — every objection traces to one of 7 unbuilt beliefs; the stated objection is rarely the real one. Diagnose which belief, don't fight the words. ([brief 03, CG])
  4. Alex Hormozi — "Objections are questions in disguise." Acknowledge → validate → answer with logic; buckets into environment/timing, other people, internal. Never steamroll. ([brief 03, AH])
  5. Sam McKenna — SMYKM element 6 — pre-empt the forthcoming objection in the message ("While you may already have a [tool]…" then refute with deeper value). Handle it before it's raised. (Apollo)

Verdict: PROVEN. Five operators (four sales closers + one enterprise-DM operator), all converging on: clarify/isolate the real objection before deploying any scripted answer, and a price objection is a belief gap. The "clarify before answering" move is the universal pattern.

Divergence — our scripted answers are good but fire before the clarify step. Copy-standard #4 and #7 are strong answers, but they deploy the rebuttal immediately. The proven consensus (Miner, Nelson) is to clarify the real objection first ("when you say it's slop, what specifically reads as slop to you?") then answer. Revise pass: bolt a one-line clarify in front of each scripted objection counter so it isn't answering an objection the prospect didn't quite make. The pre-emption move (McKenna) is also worth adding to the cold message itself.

  6. Sales.co (Doyle/Greenfeld) — $90k MRR. The offer is a thing they want ("do you want this list of all these advertisers?"), built from the prospect's own ecosystem. Lead with the artifact, not the pitch. (IndieHackers)

  8. Taylor Holiday / CTC — owns and operates brands in-house so they "speak credibly about real-time best practices"; the acquisition engine is being demonstrably good in public. The mature form of "I built the ad instead of pitching you." (skio)
  9. Eric Nowoslawski + 30MPC — both now say the offer/artifact out-performs personalization because AI commoditized personalization. The edge is a value-prop a competitor can't claim or a finished artifact that closes itself. ([brief 07 §2026])

Verdict: PROVEN, and the most cross-corroborated pattern in the entire dataset. Nine sources across cold email, DTC media-buying, music licensing, and DTC agency, plus two independent Loom case studies with hard numbers (34%, 22%, 30%). The artifact-first/permission-first structure is "not a nice-to-have, it's the lever" (brief 06). This is the single strongest thing the GTM is built on.

Divergence — permission-gate the artifact, and the artifact must be genuinely good. Two flags the revise pass must heed:

  • (a) The highest-converting form is permission-FIRST, not link-embedded. Every Loom case (150-brand, 4%→22%, the permission-ask template) asks "want me to send it?" before sending, and the permission-ask alone pulls ~14 replies/1,000 with the video converting the yes. Our copy-standard #1-3 embed the link in touch 1. (Same divergence as Tactic 5 — worth A/B testing the permission-ask variant, because the proof leans the other way.)
  • (b) Pierno's trap: his close-rate math rode on borrowed YC-logo credibility. Our proof-only, no-track-record constraint means the pre-built ad has to be genuinely good, not a demo — it carries the credibility a case study would. This is a production-quality bar, not a copy fix, but it's load-bearing.

TACTIC 11 — The channel order and budget call (warm DM first, defer the cold-email rig)

What our system does. Playbook §2 budget verdict and §5 channel orders: X/LinkedIn DMs first (near-free, zero warmup), cold-email rig deferred until a dollar earns it.

Corroborating sources:

  1. GigRadar 2026 (100M+ emails) — cost-per-meeting: cold email $150-300, LinkedIn $100-250, Upwork $20-50. Most agencies wrongly start with cold email and ignore warm-intent channels. For a $750 budget, warm-intent channels stretch the dollar 5-10x. (gigradar) Single primary source on the exact cost figures (flag).
  2. 2026 deliverability reality — Gmail/Yahoo Feb-2024 rules + the ~Nov-2025 hard-reject change mean the cold-email rig now needs authenticated subdomains, 6-8 week warmup, verified lists, complaint rate <0.1%. 78% of teams had to re-architect. A real cost to stand up. (Smartlead survey, Validity 2025)
  3. 2026 LinkedIn/X DM data — DM ("sends") is now the strongest ranking signal; X has no throttling so DM volume still works; the warm-DM lane is near-free. (hyperclapper)
  4. Instantly 2026 — LinkedIn DM ~10.3% reply vs cold email ~3.43%; reply-to-meeting LinkedIn ~35% vs cold email ~30%. Warm channels convert better and cost less. ([brief 07 §2026])
  5. Warm-beats-cold consensus — across the briefs, the warm/relevant lane (community paths, 2nd-degree, signal-matched DMs) beats cold by a wide margin; the 147,000-email volume play collapsed to 1.2% while warm-conversation replies hit 34%. (prospeo)

Verdict: STRONG (4-5 sources, one weak on exact cost numbers). The direction — warm/intent channels first, defer the expensive cold-email rig on a $750 budget — is corroborated by deliverability cost reality, the 2026 DM-channel data, and the warm-beats-cold consensus. The exact cost-per-meeting figures rest mainly on GigRadar (flag), so call this STRONG not PROVEN.

Divergence — none; the budget call is sound. The only nuance: the briefs are unanimous that multichannel (email + LinkedIn + a call) roughly 2-3x's effective response (Saleshandy: email-only 0.11% → +LinkedIn 0.21% → +call 0.28%). Our "layer ONE other channel" rule (§2) is correctly conservative for a solo founder but the data says a call leg is the biggest single add. Worth noting for when capacity allows.

Convergence summary (what's proven, what's weaker, what to change)

PROVEN (5+ independent sources) — build on these without hedging:

  • Cold-email structure: short, problem-led, single soft CTA (Tactic 1, 7 sources)
  • The opener: true + specific + them-first, curiosity over pitch (Tactic 2, 7 sources)
  • Signal/trigger targeting: WHO before WHAT, real trigger doubles-to-10x's reply (Tactic 3, 8 sources — strongest)
  • Follow-up cadence: 3-5 touches, follow-ups carry ~44%, stop at 4-6 (Tactic 4, 7 sources)
  • Soft single interest-based CTA, defer the calendar link (Tactic 5, 7 sources)
  • DM is traffic / profile closes / give-first (Tactic 6 structure, 7 sources)
  • Discovery spine: frame → gap → consequence → bridge → price-after-desire (Tactic 7, 6 sources)
  • The close: price late, anchor to their P&L, assumptive, never discount (Tactic 8, 6 sources)
  • Objection handling: clarify the real objection first, price objection = belief gap (Tactic 9, 5 sources)
  • Free-artifact / permission-first / let-it-be-the-proof (Tactic 10, 9 sources — most corroborated pattern in the file)

STRONG (3-4 sources) — trust, but not yet bulletproof:

  • Depth-over-volume DM choice for our ticket size (Tactic 6, matched to deal size)
  • The exact 13-step merged discovery call map (Tactic 7 synthesis, not yet run live)

DIVERGENCES the revise pass should resolve (ranked by impact):

  1. Permission-gate the artifact (Tactics 5 + 10). Copy-standard #1-3 embed the artifact link in touch 1; the proven higher-converting pattern across every Loom case is to ask "want me to send it?" first and deliver on the yes. Genuine tension with the show-immediately proof-first thesis — A/B it, don't assume the embedded link wins.
  2. Make "profile converts" a hard pre-launch gate (Tactic 6). Every DM source says the DM only buys a profile click and the profile closes. The system names this but has no proof the Adwield/Tideover profiles carry one offer + visible proof. Gate outbound on it.
  3. Add the two missing discovery moves (Tactic 7). Miner's consequence question (make them voice the cost of the dying winner) and Nelson's decision pre-frame in the agenda (cheapest anti-ghosting fix). Add both to v1-sales-call-kit.
  4. Clarify before answering objections (Tactic 9). Copy-standard #4/#7 fire the rebuttal immediately; bolt a one-line clarify in front ("when you say slop, what specifically reads as slop?") per Miner/Nelson.
  5. Tighten the connection-note rule (Tactic 6). Playbook §3.4 says "no note" flatly; 2026 data says "no note, OR one ultra-specific <180-char note." Small fix.
  6. Hold the line on cadence length (Tactic 4). Not a change — a guard. Every dataset contradicts the "8-12 touch" guru advice; keep 3-5, don't let a revise pad it past 6.

The one thing that would break the whole system if it drifted: letting AI write the per-prospect hook. Eight sources (Tactic 3) say token/AI personalization is now worse than none. The "tool pulls the signal, human writes the hook" rule is non-negotiable.
